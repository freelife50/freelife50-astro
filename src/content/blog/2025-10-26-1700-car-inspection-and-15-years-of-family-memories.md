<h2 id="1-is-1700-expensive-or-cheap" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>1️⃣ Is $1,700 Expensive or Cheap?</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Gemini_Generated_Image_4643ew4643ew4643.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2615"/></figure>

<p>When I saw the dealer’s estimate, I stared at the number again and again.<br><strong>Inspection fee: $1,710.</strong><strong><br></strong> Was that too high or fair? Hard to say.</p>

<p>Buying a new car would mean $400–$500 monthly payments.<br>So $1,700 is only about <strong>four months’ worth of new car payments.</strong><strong><br></strong> For that, our 15-year-old Prius can carry our family again.</p>

<p>Fifteen years ago, our eldest son was in high school and our youngest in grade school.<br>The shine on the silver body has faded, scratches have grown —<br>but every time I hold the steering wheel, I feel calm.<br>It’s as if I can still hear my family’s laughter echoing from the seats.</p>

<p>Was this $1,700 a “big expense”?<br>Or a “small investment to keep our memories alive”?</p>

<p>Let’s set emotions aside and look at the numbers.<br>Here’s what it <em>actually</em> costs to keep my 2010 Prius (ZVW30) running each year:</p>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><tbody><tr><td><strong>Item</strong></td><td><strong>Details</strong></td><td><strong>Yearly Cost</strong></td></tr><tr><td>Inspection (every 2 yrs)</td><td>$1,710 ÷ 2</td><td><strong>$855</strong></td></tr><tr><td>Auto tax</td><td>over 13 yrs / 1.8L class</td><td><strong>$260</strong></td></tr><tr><td>Insurance</td><td>50s driver, no vehicle coverage</td><td><strong>$370</strong></td></tr><tr><td>Gasoline</td><td>10,000 km/year, 22–23 km/L, $1.14/L</td><td><strong>$510</strong></td></tr><tr><td><strong>Total</strong></td><td></td><td><strong>≈ $1,995/year ($165/month)</strong></td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<p>$165 per month — that’s the “real” cost to keep Prius-chan alive.<br>Not cheap, but not outrageous either.<br>For safety, reliability, and peace of mind, it’s fair value.</p>

<h2 id="3-replace-or-maintain-the-true-value-of-" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>3️⃣ Replace or Maintain? The True Value of Family Time</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Gemini_Generated_Image_d1b6ded1b6ded1b6.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2624"/></figure>

<p>A new Prius now costs around <strong>$25,000–$30,000</strong>.<br>That’s $400–$500 per month plus higher taxes and insurance.</p>

<p>My current Prius costs <strong>$165/month</strong> — and no more loans.<br>The numbers say it all:<br>👉 <strong>Maintenance &gt; Replacement.</strong></p>

<p>But numbers don’t tell the whole story.</p>

<p>This car has witnessed our lives:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>My eldest son sitting nervously beside me on his university entrance exam day.<br></li>

<li>My youngest, sweaty from practice, saying “I’m starving” on the way home.<br></li>

<li>My mother, battling cancer, smiling softly at Nara’s Mt. Wakakusa: “It’s beautiful…”<br></li>
</ul>

<p>That scene through the windshield — I’ll never forget it.</p>

<p>$165 a month isn’t just maintenance.<br>It’s the <strong>cost of not forgetting.</strong><strong><br></strong> Prius-chan has carried all those moments — laughter, silence, and goodbyes.</p>

<h2 id="4-the-1700-breakdown--repairs-that-keep-" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>4️⃣ The $1,700 Breakdown — Repairs That Keep Her Alive</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/f7e55013a287e52a94edb27b6849b983-1.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2620"/></figure>

<p>Honestly, my first reaction was:</p>

<p>“$1,700?! That’s ridiculous!”</p>

<p>But when I dug deeper, it made sense.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><tbody><tr><td><strong>Breakdown</strong></td><td><strong>Amount</strong></td></tr><tr><td>Maintenance &amp; labor</td><td>$1,480</td></tr><tr><td>Taxes &amp; fees</td><td>$230</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Total</strong></td><td><strong>$1,710 (tax included)</strong></td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<p>Main repairs included:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Regular 24-month inspection<br></li>

<li>Brake cleaning &amp; adjustment<br></li>

<li>Engine oil &amp; coolant replacement<br></li>

<li>12V auxiliary battery replacement<br></li>

<li>A/C filter &amp; lamp replacement<br></li>

<li>💡 <strong>Front drive-shaft oil seal replacement — $310</strong><strong><br></strong></li>
</ul>

<p>At first, I didn’t even know what a “drive-shaft oil seal” was.<br>It’s a small rubber ring that keeps lubricating oil from leaking around the rotating shaft that sends power to the wheels.<br>After 15 years and 200,000 km, that rubber hardens from heat and vibration — and oil begins to seep out.</p>

<p>It’s not from damage or impact; it’s simply <strong>aging</strong>, just like human joints losing their flexibility.<br>If ignored, it can lead to major drivetrain failure — and the car won’t pass inspection.<br>So it wasn’t optional; it was <em>necessary surgery</em> to keep her alive.</p>

<p>Once I understood that, everything made sense.<br>This repair wasn’t an expense; it was a <strong>life-extension treatment</strong> for Prius-chan.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>🧠 Summary of This Chapter</strong></h3>

<p>At first, the cost felt high —<br>but once I understood the reason, it became clear: some repairs truly <em>make sense.</em><em><br></em> Both cars and humans need invisible care as they age.</p>

<p>Prius-chan and I are no longer young.<br>If we were people, we’d be at the age where regular health checkups are essential.<br>This oil seal replacement was, in a way, like removing a small polyp during a routine colon exam —<br>not urgent, but neglect it, and it could turn serious later.</p>

<p>Thinking about it that way, <strong>“preventive maintenance” is really no different from human healthcare.</strong><strong><br></strong> Both are about staying healthy enough to keep going for many more years.</p>

<p>“Can it really last 10 more years?” my wife asked.<br>“Yes — if we keep maintaining it,” I replied.</p>

<p>Prius (ZVW30, 2010) is built tough.<br>With proper care, it can reach <strong>300,000–350,000 km</strong>.</p>

<h2 id="6-fifteen-years-of-family-miles" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>6️⃣ Fifteen Years of Family Miles</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/6974f9ef6e7860bbaf3047e11b45fe1c.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2625"/></figure>

<p>We bought our Prius when our boys were 15 and 12.<br>Before that, we had a Toyota Spacio — full of camping and amusement park memories.</p>

<p>When mileage hit 100,000 km, we decided it was time for something new.<br>We looked at the Fit Shuttle and Prius — both good cars,<br>but Prius had that quiet, futuristic charm.</p>

<p>We bought a <strong>demo model for about $13,000</strong>, shiny silver.<br>As I picked it up, I said softly:</p>

<p>“Let’s go places together, partner.”</p>

<p>From that day, this car has seen everything —<br>weekend hikes, long drives to Kyoto, road trips with my late mother.</p>

<p>And it has scars too:<br>A dent from when my son hit a pole.<br>A bent license plate from when a deer jumped out of the bushes — <em>bang!</em><em><br></em> The deer stumbled off into the woods.<br>To this day, every time I see that dent, I think,</p>

<p>“I hope that deer’s okay…”</p>

<p>Those marks are memories now — proof we’ve lived.</p>
