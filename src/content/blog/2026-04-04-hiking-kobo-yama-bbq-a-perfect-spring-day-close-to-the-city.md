<p>Hi there.</p>

<p>"Do I need to be fit to go hiking?" "Do I need a lot of gear?"</p>

<p>If those thoughts have been holding you back, I totally get it.</p>

<p>But here's the thing — <strong>the right trail makes hiking accessible to anyone.</strong> You don't need to go full mountaineer mode. Just pick a course that suits your fitness level, and the mountains become a whole lot more welcoming.</p>

<p>Kōbō-yama in Hadano City, Kanagawa, is exactly that kind of place. And the cherry blossoms? Absolutely stunning.</p>

<p>In this post, I'll walk you through the full experience — from the hike itself to a BBQ at Midoriya Farm — based on what we actually did.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Anyone curious about hiking but not sure if they're fit enough</li>

<p><strong>Kōbō-yama is one of the best beginner-friendly hiking courses I've come across.</strong></p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Gear? Just Your Phone, Your Wallet, and a Card</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/Gemini_Generated_Image_m84bzwm84bzwm84b.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3341"/></figure>

<p>Let me be straight with you.</p>

<p>All I brought that day was <strong>my phone, my wallet, and a card.</strong></p>

<p>I did have a backpack — but it was basically empty. It was just there to carry the groceries I'd buy at AEON after the hike.</p>

<p>That's the beauty of this course. <strong>You do your food shopping after the hike, not before.</strong> Walk the mountain light, then swing by AEON on the way to Midoriya Farm.</p>

<p>And Kōbō-yama is <strong>right next to town</strong> — you step off the train and you're basically already on the trail.</p>

<p>So if you've been putting this off because you thought you needed to pack a ton of stuff — you don't. <strong>You can literally show up with next to nothing.</strong></p>

<p>That said — one important note:</p>

<p><strong>Don't underestimate the mountain.</strong></p>

<p>Always check the weather before you go. Wear shoes you can actually walk in. And bring at least a little water, just in case.</p>

<p>Being light and being careless are two different things. <strong>"Minimal prep, but never zero awareness"</strong> — that's the right approach here.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Tsurumaki-onsen Station — Cherry Blossoms Before You Even Start</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3121.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3340"/></figure>

<p>Step off the Odakyu Line at Tsurumaki-onsen Station, and there they are — cherry blossoms, right in front of you.</p>

<p>Soft pink petals framing the station sign against a blue sky.</p>

<p>"We came to the right place" — that thought hit me before I'd even taken a single step on the trail.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Trail Difficulty — Genuinely Manageable for Beginners</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3144.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3342"/></figure>

<p>Watch the Short Video 🎬</p>

<p>👉 <a href="https://www.youtube.com/shorts/oBIUAiM8pfw">Watch the short video from Kōbō-yama here</a></p>

<p>The Kōbō-yama hiking course is part of the <strong>Kanto Fureai no Michi</strong> trail network, officially named <strong>"The Path of Kōbō Daishi and Cherry Blossoms."</strong></p>

<p>The route runs from Tsurumaki-onsen Station through Gongen-yama, Kōbō-yama, and Azuma-yama — <strong>about 9.4 km in total.</strong></p>

<p>Key points on difficulty:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>No steep rock faces or chain sections</strong></li>

<li><strong>Well-maintained, easy-to-follow paths</strong></li>

<li><strong>Sneakers worked just fine for several in our group</strong></li>

<li><strong>Enough ups and downs to feel satisfying without being brutal</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Our group was about 20 people — parents who first met when our kids were in high school together. Some hadn't hiked in years. Everyone made it through, chatting the whole way.</p>

<p>Think of it less as a mountain climb and more as <strong>a long walk through the forest</strong> — that's probably the closest analogy.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The History — Legends of Kōbō Daishi on the Summit</strong></h2>

<p>The mountain takes its name from <strong>Kōbō Daishi (Kūkai)</strong>, the revered Buddhist monk who is said to have trained here during the Heian period.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Legend of the "Milk Well"</strong></h3>

<div class="wp-block-columns"><div class="wp-block-column"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3141.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3343"/></figure>
</div>

<div class="wp-block-column"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large is-resized"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3139.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3344" style="aspect-ratio:0.7480644675402922;width:221px;height:auto"/></figure>
</div>
</div>

<p>According to local legend, a mother who couldn't produce milk came to Kōbō Daishi for help. He struck the ground with his staff, and a milky-white water began to flow.</p>

<p>It was believed that cooking rice porridge with this water would help nursing mothers, and well into the early Shōwa era, women traveled from afar to collect it.</p>

<p>The well is still there today — an old hand pump sitting on a mossy rock, water flowing through a bamboo spout.</p>

<p>A place that's been here for over a thousand years, still quietly doing its thing.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The "Time Bell" Belfry</strong></h3>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/image.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3345"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Screenshot</figcaption></figure>

<p>There's also a belfry on the summit with roots going back to the Edo period.</p>

<p>Completed in 1757, cracked in a fire, recast in 1801 with support from the Tokugawa clan, and used as a "time bell" for local residents until 1956.</p>

<p>Reading little history panels like this while you walk is unexpectedly enjoyable. <strong>Your body is moving, but so is your mind.</strong></p>

<p>At 11:15, we hit AEON for groceries. Shopping for 20 people together turned out to be half the fun.</p>

<p>By noon, we arrived at <strong>Midoriya Farm.</strong></p>

<p>The entrance sign is stylish, and there was a board out front: <strong>"Strawberries available inside."</strong> Buying strawberries after a BBQ — very on-brand for spring.</p>

<p>We had <strong>huge prawns lined up across the grill</strong>, smoke rising, everyone laughing. Three hours of good food and good company.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Ramen Stall Right Next to the BBQ — Don't Skip It</strong></h3>

<div class="wp-block-columns"><div class="wp-block-column"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3153.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3350"/></figure>

<div class="wp-block-column"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/image-1.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3351"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Screenshot</figcaption></figure>

<p>Inside Midoriya Farm, right next to the BBQ area, there's a <strong>Chinese-style noodle stall.</strong></p>

<p>"Hadano Spring Water — Housemade Noodles — Chūka Soba"</p>

<p>Cup size ¥400 (approx. $2.50) / Bowl ¥700 (approx. $4.40) / Large ¥1,200 (approx. $7.50).</p>

<p>The guy running the stall was a friendly soul.</p>

<p>While we were deep into BBQ mode, he wandered over and <strong>handed us small paper cups filled with just the soup broth</strong> — no noodles, just a taste.</p>

<p>It had this <strong>clean, gentle flavor — almost like Okinawa soba.</strong></p>

<p>"Oh, that's good" — and just like that, several people got up to order a full bowl.</p>

<p>It's steps away from the BBQ area. After a hike and a cookout, it's the perfect way to finish the day.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Bus Info for the Return</strong></h2>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Route:</strong> Kanachū Bus [Hata 26] bound for Hadano Station</li>

<li><strong>Journey time:</strong> approx. 18–20 minutes</li>

<li><strong>Fare:</strong> ¥250 (approx. $1.57)</li>
</ul>
