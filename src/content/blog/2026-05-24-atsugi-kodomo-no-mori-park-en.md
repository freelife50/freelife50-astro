<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p>Hi, I'm Hiroshi.</p>

<p>"It's a kids' park. How serious could it be?"</p>

<p>That was roughly the attitude I had when we went. On the way home, I sat in the car quietly for a while.</p>

<p>Our destination was Atsugi Kodomo no Mori Park in Atsugi, Kanagawa Prefecture. I went with my wife and our Shiba Inu, Momiji, age three. The name literally means "children's forest." So I didn't exactly arrive with the mindset of an explorer.</p>

<p>I underestimated it.</p>

<p>On the drive home, my wife and I looked at each other and said, "That was much better than expected." A few hours earlier, I had been standing at the top of a 106-meter forest slide, quietly putting my hands together like I was asking for safe passage.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-eyecatch.jpg" alt="A large tree and bench on Kunugi Hill, with Shiba Inu Momiji in the foreground" class="wp-image-6200"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">I thought it was just a kids' park. It turned out to be a proper satoyama walk</figcaption></figure>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>Who this post is for</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>People looking for a dog-friendly walking spot around Atsugi</li>
<li>Couples in their 50s who want an easy nature walk</li>
<li>Anyone scouting a park before visiting with children or grandchildren</li>
<li>People who assume a children's park will be boring for adults</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Bottom line</strong></p>

<p>Atsugi Kodomo no Mori Park is not just a playground with a few trees around it. It is a park that uses the shape of the satoyama landscape itself: hills, ponds, fields, forest paths, and raised walkways. It works surprisingly well for a relaxed walk with a dog, and it is absolutely enough for a 50-something couple's afternoon outing. Just don't underestimate the forest slide. It looks like a children's attraction, but it has a quiet talent for shaving down middle-aged confidence.</p>

<h2 id="basic-info" class="wp-block-heading">Basic Info First, Then the Real Story</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-map.jpg" alt="Guide map at the entrance of Atsugi Kodomo no Mori Park" class="wp-image-6201"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The guide map near the entrance. This is where I realized the park was bigger than expected</figcaption></figure>

<p>Before the personal story, here are the practical details.</p>

<p>Atsugi Kodomo no Mori Park is located at 916-2 Nakaogino, Atsugi City, Kanagawa Prefecture, next to Ogino Athletic Park. Admission is free, and parking is free. Opening hours are 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM from April through September, and 9:00 AM to 4:00 PM from October through March. By car, it is about 18 minutes from the Ken-O Expressway Atsugi IC. Parking Lot 2 is closest to the entrance. On weekends and long holidays, arriving early is the safer choice.</p>

<p><strong>That's the basic info. Now the real story begins.</strong></p>

<h2 id="satoyama" class="wp-block-heading">It Took Less Than Five Minutes to Realize This Was Satoyama</h2>

<p>After passing through the entrance gate, a large guide board stands right in front of you.</p>

<p>The first thing I noticed from the map was that this park was not flat.</p>

<p>There are valleys. Hills. Ponds. Rice paddies.</p>

<p>"Ah. This is a park where you walk through satoyama itself."</p>

<p>The image I had in my mind, sitting on a bench with canned coffee, disappeared without making a sound.</p>

<div style="display:grid;grid-template-columns:repeat(auto-fit,minmax(240px,1fr));gap:14px;align-items:start;">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-volcanic-layer.jpg" alt="Information board explaining the Aira-Tanzawa volcanic ash layer" class="wp-image-6203"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A board about volcanic ash layers. Supposedly for children, but I was the one stopping to read it</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-soil-layer.jpg" alt="Visible soil layer section inside the park" class="wp-image-6204"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A visible soil layer. The park does a good job turning geology into something you can actually see</figcaption></figure>

<p>Soon after we started walking, I found an explanatory panel about the Aira-Tanzawa volcanic ash layer. It said that ash from a huge eruption in Kyushu more than 30,000 years ago remains in this soil. Another panel explained Tokyo pumice and the history of volcanic activity around Hakone.</p>

<p>It was probably designed for children. I was the one standing there reading it carefully. Meanwhile, Momiji had her nose pressed to the ground, conducting her own investigation.</p>

<p>Honestly, I am not sure which of us was using the park more intelligently.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-momiji-front.jpg" alt="Shiba Inu Momiji standing near the entrance of the park" class="wp-image-6205"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Momiji had no interest in geology. Her research topic was smell</figcaption></figure>

<h2 id="corridor" class="wp-block-heading">The Forest Sky Corridor, the Pond, and a Married Couple's Silence</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-corridor-entrance.jpg" alt="Entrance sign and deck for the Forest Sky Corridor" class="wp-image-6206"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The entrance to the Forest Sky Corridor. This is where the little adventure feeling begins</figcaption></figure>

<p>Following the signs, we reached the entrance to the Forest Sky Corridor. It stretches 745 meters through the forest and rises up to 10 meters above the ground. It feels less like a normal park path and more like walking through the trees.</p>

<p>My wife, wearing her hat, started ahead of me.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-corridor-deck.jpg" alt="A long raised wooden deck running through the forest" class="wp-image-6207"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The corridor through the trees. It is higher than it looks at first</figcaption></figure>

<p>As we moved forward, I could see the ground through the trees. The pond below was much farther down than I expected. By the time I realized how high we were, we were already well into the corridor.</p>

<p>I don't know whether my wife had noticed the height or not. I didn't ask.</p>

<p>When you have been married for a while, there are moments when you talk about everything, and moments when you simply walk in the same direction without saying much. This corridor was the second kind.</p>

<p>The leaves moved. The deck made a quiet sound under our feet. I looked down, then at my wife's back, then down again. A man in his 50s can be mature in many areas of life and still become very quiet at a height of 10 meters.</p>

<div style="display:grid;grid-template-columns:repeat(auto-fit,minmax(240px,1fr));gap:14px;align-items:start;">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-pond-sign.jpg" alt="Information board for the pond below the forest corridor" class="wp-image-6208"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The pond sign below. This is where I realized the corridor was not exactly low</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-pond-view.jpg" alt="A pond surrounded by forest inside Atsugi Kodomo no Mori Park" class="wp-image-6209"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A pond surrounded by forest. This feels more like satoyama than an urban playground</figcaption></figure>
</div>

<p>We could also see rice paddies. Real rice paddies inside the park. The area is used for nature and farming programs, and the fields are arranged along the valley.</p>

<p>I honestly thought, "I didn't know Atsugi had a place like this."</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 id="slide" class="wp-block-heading">The Forest Slide: I Came in Confident, Then Quietly Lost</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-slide.jpg" alt="A long forest slide running down through the trees" class="wp-image-6210"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The forest slide. In person, it has more presence than the photo suggests</figcaption></figure>

<p>The park's Forest Slide is 106 meters long.</p>

<p>I had heard that before we went. "One hundred and six meters? Sure." That was my attitude. It is a children's park, I thought. It may be long, but it cannot be scary.</p>

<p>That was a serious miscalculation.</p>

<p>I climbed to the top and looked down. The slide curved away into the forest. I could not see the end. I had no idea how far it actually went.</p>

<p>"Ah. This is a different kind of thing."</p>

<p>I said it out loud. I am grateful no one was behind me.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-slide-tower.jpg" alt="Tower leading up to the start of the forest slide" class="wp-image-6211"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The tower leading to the start. This is where middle-aged pride gets tested quietly</figcaption></figure>

<p>Standing there, I put my hands together for just a moment.</p>

<p>A man in his 50s, praying in front of a children's slide. It was not my proudest moment, but I was more concerned about the sound of my own heartbeat.</p>

<p>The moment I started sliding, I understood. It was fast.</p>

<p><strong>Is this speed really okay?</strong></p>

<p>The question ran through my head. Before I could answer it, the slide was over.</p>

<p>It was genuinely scary.</p>

<p>I decided never to use the phrase "just for kids" carelessly again. The park returned my arrogance with interest.</p>

<p>One practical note: the slide may close during rain, high temperatures, or icy conditions. In summer, it can be closed to prevent burns. Check the official notices before visiting if the slide is one of your main goals.</p>

<h2 id="momiji" class="wp-block-heading">Momiji First, My Wife Ahead, Me at the Back</h2>

<p>After the slide, we kept walking.</p>

<p>Many of the paths inside the park are gravel, dirt, or log steps. It feels close to a light forest trail. Still, the paths are well maintained, and they were easier to walk than I expected. Sturdy shoes are a good idea.</p>

<div style="display:grid;grid-template-columns:repeat(auto-fit,minmax(240px,1fr));gap:14px;align-items:start;">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-momiji-trail.jpg" alt="Shiba Inu Momiji walking along a dirt forest path" class="wp-image-6212"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Momiji on the dirt path. Her nose was much busier than on an ordinary walk</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-log-steps.jpg" alt="Log steps on a forest path inside the park" class="wp-image-6213"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Log steps on the trail. Well maintained, but definitely more forest than sidewalk</figcaption></figure>
</div>

<p>On a slope with log steps, Momiji pulled the leash forward. My wife was walking ahead of her.</p>

<p>Momiji first, my wife ahead, and me at the back. Somehow, that order felt like the usual shape of the three of us.</p>

<p>This kind of path seems to suit dogs. Momiji's nose was constantly working, and her steps were light. At times, it felt less like I was walking her and more like she was walking me.</p>

<p>We passed several people walking their dogs. I could see why this park has become part of local dog-walking life. Dirt paths, deep greenery, and far more smells than a normal paved walk. Momiji looked different here.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-trail-sign.jpg" alt="Trail sign and forest path toward Kunugi Hill" class="wp-image-6214"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">There are signs, but the forest feeling remains</figcaption></figure>

<p>If you visit with a dog, keep the leash short and stay on the paths. This is a natural park, so insects, wild animals, and basic trail manners all matter. It feels free, but there are rules for a reason.</p>

<h2 id="kunugi" class="wp-block-heading">Time Slowed Down on Kunugi Hill</h2>

<p>After climbing a set of steep log steps, the view suddenly opened up.</p>

<p>A large kunugi oak stood there with a thick trunk and wide branches. Beneath it was a bench. The grass was bright, the forest stood beyond it, and the ridge line in the distance looked soft under the sky.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-lawn-momiji.jpg" alt="Shiba Inu Momiji walking across a grassy open area" class="wp-image-6215"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">When the path opened into grass, Momiji's step seemed to get lighter</figcaption></figure>

<p>My wife walked slowly ahead. Momiji stood beside me, looking up toward the tree.</p>

<p>We did not say anything, but it was not a bad silence.</p>

<p>It felt good. Truly.</p>

<p>A little earlier, I had been praying in front of a slide. Now I was standing quietly on a hill. Maybe these small, good moments are not as rare as we think. Maybe they are just lying around somewhere, waiting for us to stop under a tree.</p>

<h2 id="notes" class="wp-block-heading">What to Know Before You Go</h2>

<p>Long sleeves, long pants, and insect repellent are a good idea. The park has salt posts for dealing with leeches, which are active roughly from March to November.</p>

<div style="display:grid;grid-template-columns:repeat(auto-fit,minmax(240px,1fr));gap:14px;align-items:start;">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-salt-post.jpg" alt="Salt post with a warning sign about leeches" class="wp-image-6218"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A salt post for leeches. Seeing this makes you stand a little straighter</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-monkey-sign.jpg" alt="Warning sign for monkeys inside the park" class="wp-image-6219"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A monkey warning sign. This is still a natural environment</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-boar-sign.jpg" alt="Warning sign for wild boars inside the park" class="wp-image-6220"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A wild boar warning sign. For me, the slide was still scarier</figcaption></figure>
</div>

<p>There were also signs warning about wild boars. To be honest, the slide still scared me more. That says more about the slide than about the boars.</p>

<p>The entrance area is paved and easy to move through, but much of the park is gravel, dirt, and stairs. It is not a place where you can comfortably push a stroller through every area. Bringing drinks and food is sensible. Vending machines are available near the management building and the work hut.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/atsugi-kodomo-no-mori-rules.jpg" alt="Information board showing park rules and opening hours" class="wp-image-6221"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Opening hours and rules are posted near the entrance. <h2 id="ending" class="wp-block-heading">About That Quiet Drive Home</h2>

<p>At the beginning, I wrote that I was quiet in the car on the way home.</p>

<p>Why? There was no dramatic reason. I think it was simply that kind of time.</p>

<p>We went to a children's park. It surprised us in the best way. I got genuinely scared by a slide. I stood on a hill and did nothing for a while. Then we went home.</p>

<p>It was a day when a park I had underestimated quietly defeated me.</p>

<p>Momiji probably did not think about any of that. She was most likely just happy.</p>

<p>I felt a little jealous of that.</p>

<p>I would go again. Next time, I am bringing lunch.</p>
