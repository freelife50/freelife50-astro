<h2 id="chapter-1｜furusato-nozei-is-a-good-syste" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Chapter 1｜Furusato Nozei Is a Good System – Or It Was</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Google_AI_Studio_2025-07-26T01_19_08.064Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2010"/></figure>

<p>Furusato Nozei is, at its core, a good idea.<br>Even if you live in the city, you can choose to support local communities through your taxes.<br>Instead of your taxes being taken without say, you could pick which areas to support. That kind of “choice” is empowering.<br>The return gifts weren’t just freebies — they sparked curiosity about those regions. A delicious gift often led to actual visits and travel.</p>

<p>And the rules were clear:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Return gifts worth up to 30% of the donation<br></li>

<li>¥2,000 self-pay only<br></li>

<li>Tax deductions within set limits<br></li>
</ul>

<p>In other words, it was a well-balanced win-win system: You support local areas <strong>and</strong> gain something in return.<br>I used to enjoy picking gifts with my family every year, thinking, “This system really works.”</p>

<p>But then… came <strong>that notice.</strong></p>

<p>To be honest, I just wanted to get back a little from the taxes I was forced to pay.<br>Why should that be called “wrong”?<br>Why must we always lose quietly?</p>

<h2 id="chapter-2｜the-system-quietly-changed-in-" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Chapter 2｜The System Quietly Changed in Fall 2024</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Google_AI_Studio_2025-07-26T01_20_51.466Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2011"/></figure>

<p>In October 2024, the Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications issued a notice banning point rewards for Furusato Nozei.</p>

<p>The official reason?<br><strong>“Excessive competition undermines the spirit of the system.”</strong><strong><br></strong> That sounds reasonable — but it felt off.</p>

<p>Point rewards were <strong>the reason</strong> many chose certain donation platforms.<br>Suddenly calling it “against the system’s purpose”? That shift felt more like <strong>a signal</strong> — something was changing behind the scenes.</p>

<p>Platforms like Rakuten Furusato Nozei, which excelled in point-based marketing, were hit hard.</p>

<p>Here’s what happened after the ban:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Citizens</strong>: Lost their points — i.e., lost part of their tax return<br></li>

<li><strong>Platforms</strong>: Lost their competitive edge<br></li>

<li><strong>Local governments</strong>: Still paid high fees, but had less appeal to donors<br></li>
</ul>

<p>If points were banned but <strong>excessive handling fees</strong> were left untouched…<br>Then clearly, <strong>someone else is benefiting</strong> from this.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 id="chapter-3｜who-gained-and-who-lost" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Chapter 3｜Who Gained and Who Lost?</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Google_AI_Studio_2025-07-26T01_22_22.717Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2012"/></figure>

<p><strong>Winners:</strong></p>

<p>✔️ <strong>Intermediary companies (platforms)</strong><strong><br></strong> They previously covered part of the point rewards. Now, with the ban:<br>→ Their <strong>profit margins increased</strong>.</p>

<p><strong>Losers:</strong></p>

<p>❌ <strong>Citizens</strong>:<br>Used to get real value back through points. Now, with only the gift, the ¥2,000 self-pay feels heavier.</p>

<p>❌ <strong>Local governments</strong>:<br>Still paying up to 20% of donation value as fees to platforms.<br>With fewer donations post-ban, they <strong>lose funds</strong> they could’ve used freely.</p>

<p>❌ <strong>Point-driven platforms like Rakuten</strong>:<br>They built strategies around point-based competition. With that gone, their strengths are nullified.</p>

<p>➡️ In short:<br>Those who thrived on point competition were crushed.<br><strong>Only unrelated businesses gained.</strong><strong><br></strong> And for us? It’s not just “bad vibes” — we <strong>truly lost value</strong> in our taxes.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 id="chapter-4｜points-are-banned-fees-stay-do" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Chapter 4｜Points Are Banned. Fees Stay. Does That Make Sense?</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Google_AI_Studio_2025-07-26T01_23_44.879Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2013"/></figure>

<p>This ban was led by the Ministry of Internal Affairs, especially by <strong>Yuya Hasegawa</strong>, Assistant Section Chief of the Municipal Tax Division.<br>He’s been quoted saying:<br><strong>“Using points or gifts for tax-saving is against the system’s purpose.”</strong></p>

<p>Fine. But what about the <strong>excessive fees</strong> behind the scenes?</p>

<p>Example:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Donation of ¥50,000 → Up to ¥10,000 (20%) in fees<br></li>

<li>Services include listing, payment handling, shipping support<br></li>

<li>Yet, local governments <strong>can’t choose platforms freely</strong><strong><br></strong></li>

<li>Some even <strong>covertly paid for points</strong> despite “company-funded” claims<br></li>
</ul>

<p>So now:<br><strong>Points banned = OK. But fees untouched = Also OK?</strong></p>

<p>That’s not “reform.” That’s selective censorship.</p>

<p>Hiding behind the term “system improvement” while leaving deep-rooted distortions intact is unacceptable.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 id="chapter-5｜who-gave-the-final-green-light" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Chapter 5｜Who Gave the Final Green Light?</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Google_AI_Studio_2025-07-26T01_25_25.960Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2014"/></figure>

<p>The final stamp of approval came from then Minister of Internal Affairs: <strong>Takamasa Matsumoto (LDP)</strong>.</p>

<p>Even if an assistant manager led the policy, it wouldn’t have passed without <strong>ministerial approval</strong>.<br>So is this only a bureaucratic issue?<br>No. It’s political too.</p>

<p>Let’s break it down:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Citizens: Lost benefits<br></li>

<li>Local governments: Lost donations<br></li>

<li>Platforms: Lost marketing tools<br></li>

<li>Intermediary firms: Gained profits<br></li>

<li>Politicians: Gained “reform points,” appeased fiscal interests, ensured future corporate support<br></li>
</ul>

<p>In other words, this was a politically <strong>convenient reform</strong>.<br>They protected profits and suppressed dissent — quietly.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Supplemental｜How Bureaucrats Quietly Benefited</strong></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Bureaucrats didn’t pocket cash directly.<br>But they <strong>gained influence and future perks</strong>.</p>

<ol class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Amakudari (post-retirement jobs)</strong><strong><br></strong> Some platform companies hired ex-bureaucrats as “advisors.”<br>Friendly regulations today = high-level jobs tomorrow.<br></li>

<li><strong>Reform results for promotion</strong><strong><br></strong> “Fixing the system” = Good on paper = Promotion<br></li>

<li><strong>Responsibility dodging</strong><strong><br></strong> Points = Banned<br>High fees = Ignored<br>→ Convenient for both companies <strong>and</strong> officials<br></li>
</ol>

<p>That’s the core of this <strong>quiet extraction mechanism</strong>.</p>

<h2 id="chapter-6｜who-is-this-system-really-for" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Chapter 6｜Who Is This System Really For?</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Google_AI_Studio_2025-07-26T01_26_47.861Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2015"/></figure>

<p>Furusato Nozei was supposed to let us <strong>support regions we care about</strong>.<br>But now, it looks like this:<br><strong>A mechanism where tax money flows indirectly to corporations.</strong></p>

<p>Return gone. Burden stays. Profits remain.</p>

<p>Let’s be clear — <strong>not all companies are bad</strong>.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><tbody><tr><td><strong>Platform</strong></td><td><strong>Point Usage</strong></td><td><strong>Impact of Ban</strong></td></tr><tr><td>Rakuten / au PAY</td><td>Gave points to users</td><td>Major damage. Lost competitive edge</td></tr><tr><td>Satofull / Furunavi / Furusato Choice</td><td>Minimal or no points</td><td>Less affected, even gained advantage</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<p>What’s wrong is <strong>targeting only point-based competition</strong> while keeping <strong>the fee business untouched</strong>.<br>That’s not reform — that’s <strong>selective enforcement</strong>.</p>

<p>We wanted to <strong>support</strong>.<br>Now we’re just being <strong>quietly harvested</strong>.</p>

<h2 id="chapter-7｜this-is-why-i-choose-to-speak-" class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Chapter 7｜This Is Why I Choose to Speak Up</strong></h2>

<p>I’m not rejecting the Furusato Nozei system.<br>Used right, it’s still a great idea.</p>

<p>But this shift —<br>Where no one takes responsibility, and only the public quietly loses —<br>That’s not something I can overlook.</p>

<p>Too many people are unaware of how they’re <strong>being quietly shaved away</strong>.<br>And in other areas too — like <strong>iDeCo (private pension savings)</strong>:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Touted as tax-saving<br></li>

<li>But withdrawal now taxed<br></li>

<li>Payouts now stretched to 10 years instead of 5<br>→ Less chance to fully use retirement tax breaks<br></li>
</ul>

<p>Politicians can be voted out.<br>Bureaucrats? Their names stay hidden.</p>

<p>We need accountability.<br>And if the politicians won’t hold them accountable, we <strong>must vote accordingly</strong>.</p>

<p>This is not just a system update.<br>This is <strong>a quiet shift of wealth and responsibility</strong>, taken from the people and handed to power.</p>
