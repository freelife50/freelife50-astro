<p>Welcome to the <strong>not-Tokyo Mt. Takao</strong>.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>“I Went to Mt. Takao This Weekend.”</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large is-resized"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/image.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3048" style="width:626px;height:auto"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Screenshot</figcaption></figure>

<p>Say that, and <strong>100 out of 100 people</strong> will picture the famous mountain in Hachioji — cable cars, dumplings, and crowds.</p>

<p>But that’s <strong>not</strong> where I went.</p>

<p>I went to <strong>Mt. Takao in Yokohama</strong>.</p>

<p>It’s in Midori Ward, Yokohama City.<br>Elevation? <strong>About 100 meters.</strong><br>(100.46m if you want to be precise.)</p>

<p>You might think:<br><strong>“That’s… low.”</strong> 😅</p>

<p>I thought so too.<br>But after walking it, I realized this wasn’t just a stroll —<br>it turned into a <strong>surprisingly rich little journey</strong>.</p>

<p>Today, I’ll walk you through the <strong>13km traverse from Tōkaichiba Station to Suzukakedai Station</strong>,<br>along with updates from my middle-aged lower back (important).</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Route &amp; Stats (From My Activity Log)</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/image-1.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3046"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Screenshot</figcaption></figure>

<p><strong>🗺 About the Route</strong> Google Maps makes it look like a single path, but I actually explored all over the forest, as shown in the tracking log. The map shows the “shortest route” (approx. 10km), but with fun detours, it becomes a satisfying 13km hike with over 400m of elevation gain!</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full is-resized"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/image-4.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-3070" style="aspect-ratio:1.1453655242275873;width:371px;height:auto"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Screenshot</figcaption></figure>

<p><strong>Route:</strong><br>JR Tōkaichiba Station<br>→ Niiharu Citizen’s Forest<br>→ Miho Citizen’s Forest<br>→ Mt. Takao (Iizuna Shrine)<br>→ Tokyu Suzukakedai Station</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Distance:</strong> approx. 13.0 km</li>

<li><strong>Time:</strong> 3 hours 23 minutes (including breaks)</li>

<li><strong>Total ascent:</strong> <strong>412 meters</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>“Wait — how do you climb over 400 meters on a 100-meter mountain?”</p>

<p>Good question. I said the same thing.</p>

<p>This route constantly dips and rises through <strong>yato terrain</strong> — small valleys and ridges —<br>so the elevation gain stacks up fast.</p>

<p><strong>In total, you climb more than Tokyo Tower’s height under your own power.</strong></p>

<p>Lesson learned:<br><strong>Don’t underestimate low mountains. My back didn’t.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Course Breakdown: From Satoyama to Sacred Ground</strong></h2>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Start → Niiharu Citizen’s Forest</strong></h3>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/F25C7512-B859-4DE8-AD02-B31C0FDDDDFF.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-3049"/></figure>

<p>Leave Tōkaichiba Station, walk a bit, and suddenly you’re inside the forest.</p>

<p>And your brain goes:<br><strong>“Wait… this is still Yokohama?”</strong></p>

<p>Dirt paths, thick trees, wetlands —<br>a preserved <strong>satoyama</strong> landscape that feels untouched.</p>

<p>It’s not over-manicured, and that’s the charm.<br>Just breathing the air here already feels like a win.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Forest Relay: Niiharu → Miho</strong></h3>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/AFCF826A-AD01-4D8C-957B-F63684FD3B72.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-3051"/></figure>

<p>After Niiharu, a short stretch of road leads into <strong>Miho Citizen’s Forest</strong>.</p>

<p>This is one of the best parts of the route:<br><strong>green spaces connected like stepping stones.</strong></p>

<p>Miho’s ridge trail is especially pleasant.<br>You expect to pass other hikers and exchange greetings…</p>

<p>But I didn’t see a single soul.</p>

<p>For a moment I wondered:<br><strong>“Am I… sealed inside a barrier?”</strong> 😆</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Summit: Mt. Takao &amp; Iizuna Shrine</strong></h3>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/82A6520A-E7D4-4B29-B1E2-D980AF451044.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-3052"/></figure>

<p>After a steeper climb and some stairs, you reach the highlight —<br><strong>Iizuna Shrine at the summit.</strong></p>

<p>Here’s where a long-held mystery clicked for me:</p>

<p>Why is there a Mt. Takao in Yokohama?</p>

<p>Turns out, this shrine was officially <strong>enshrined from Mt. Takao in Hachioji</strong> —<br>sharing the same deity, Iizuna Gongen.</p>

<p>So this isn’t a copy.<br><strong>It’s a legitimate branch — a proper “norenn-wake.”</strong></p>

<p>And there’s more.</p>

<p>This spot also hosted a <strong>first-class triangulation point</strong> in the Meiji era.<br>Before GPS existed, <strong>Japan’s national maps were measured from here.</strong></p>

<p>Standing there, I thought:<br><strong>“So this is where Japan was measured.”</strong></p>

<p>That realization gives the small shrine a surprisingly heavy presence.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>A Perfect View… With One Small Complaint</strong></h3>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/73C0A3C9-2BF2-4005-9112-5DE12E4F08A6.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-3055"/></figure>

<p>From the summit, the view opens wide.</p>

<p><strong>The entire Tanzawa mountain range spreads across the horizon.</strong><br>Fields below, city beyond.</p>

<p>(Seeing a Nitori sign adds a weirdly comforting touch of daily life.)</p>

<p>But let me be honest.</p>

<p><strong>That one tower mansion… yeah, it’s kind of in the way.</strong> 😂</p>

<p>Still, the more I stared, the more it felt right.</p>

<p>Nature.<br>City.<br>Mountains.<br>A single high-rise.</p>

<p><strong>This mix is also part of modern Japan’s scenery.</strong></p>

<p>Sit on the bench, eat an onigiri, crack a beer…<br>Yeah, that would’ve been perfect.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Finish Line: Suzukakedai Station</strong></h3>

<p>After soaking in the view, it’s downhill.</p>

<p>Through the shrine approach, across Route 246, and you’re at Suzukakedai Station.</p>

<p>After 13km, the asphalt hits your legs hard —<br>but the <strong>sense of completion</strong> is solid.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Real Finish Line</strong></h3>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large is-resized"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/DEC965D6-7F2E-487E-9F3A-AAB404741983.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-3054" style="width:560px;height:auto"/></figure>

<p>Let’s be honest.</p>

<p>All the history and scenery aside,<br><strong>my true goal was this: a cold beer after the hike.</strong></p>

<p>Clinking glasses at the station with my walking buddies,<br>I swear I heard my cells whisper:<br><strong>“We’ve been waiting for this.”</strong></p>

<p>After 13km,<br><strong>beer beats any luxury wine.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>A Few Words From a Middle-Aged Hiker</strong></h2>

<p>Learned the hard way, so you don’t have to:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Wear proper shoes</strong><br>This is not a park stroll. Dirt, roots, mud — sneakers at minimum.</li>

<li><strong>Use a hiking map app</strong><br>Google Maps can struggle in the forest.<br>Apps like <strong>YAMAP</strong> handle the Niiharu–Miho–Takao routes well.</li>

<li><strong>Plan toilets &amp; snacks</strong><br>Toilets exist, but no shops inside the forests.<br>Buy water and snacks near the station first.<br>And yes — it’s still the city. You can’t just “handle it anywhere.” 😅</li>
</ul>
