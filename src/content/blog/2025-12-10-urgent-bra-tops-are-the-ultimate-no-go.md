<p><strong>A Radiologic Technologist’s Honest Plea:<br>“Please… just come in with a plain T-shirt!”<br>— The Truth Behind the Mysterious “Staircase Shadows” on Chest X-rays</strong></p>

<p><strong><br></strong> Reading time: approx. 5 minutes</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong><span class="fz-20px">Who Should Read This</span></strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Anyone scheduled for a chest X-ray at a workplace or community health check<br></li>

<li>Anyone who asks, <em>“Can I keep my bra on?”</em> every year<br></li>

<li>Anyone who believes <em>“Camisoles and bra tops should be fine, right?”</em><em><br></em></li>

<li>Anyone who wants to avoid retakes, extra radiation, and wasted time<br></li>

<li>Anyone curious about what radiology staff silently struggle with every year<br></li>
</ul>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Conclusion: A Single T-shirt Is the Most Powerful Outfit on Earth for Chest X-rays</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Image_fx-2.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2884"/></figure>

<p>No metal.<br>No plastic.<br>No compression.</p>

<p>Clothes that meet these <strong>three rules</strong> produce the <strong>clearest, most diagnostic-quality image</strong>.</p>

<p>Bras, bra tops, camisoles…<br><strong>All of them are officially in the “Hall of NG (No-Good) Items.”</strong></p>

<p>Why? Because:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Compression marks create shadows that <em>shouldn’t</em> exist on your body<br></li>

<li>Metal or plastic parts appear as foreign objects<br></li>
</ul>

<p>In short:<br><strong>These shadows can lower the diagnostic quality of your image.</strong></p>

<p>And the infamous artifact mentioned in the title—<br><strong>the “mysterious staircase”</strong>—<br>This is the ultimate trouble caused by bra tops.</p>

<p>Let’s walk through the reasons clearly from the perspective of the imaging room.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Season Every Radiologic Technologist Secretly Dreads</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Image_fx-3.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2885"/></figure>

<p>When health-check season arrives, techs quietly brace themselves:</p>

<p><em>(Inner voice: “Here comes the bra problem again…</em><em><br></em><em> Please… no bra tops today…")</em></p>

<p>Every year, we hear the same questions:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>“Is it okay to keep my bra on?”<br></li>

<li>“My camisole only has tiny plastic parts—should be fine, right?”<br></li>

<li>“My bra top doesn’t have metal, so it’s OK?”<br></li>
</ul>

<p>We’ve heard these questions literally <strong>thousands of times</strong>.<br>But here’s the truth:</p>

<p><strong>The moment there is compression, it’s already NG.</strong></p>

<p>The skin gets squeezed,<br>creating <strong>unnatural lines</strong> — like someone drew stripes with a marker.<br>These lines show up <em>very clearly</em> on X-rays.</p>

<p>Patients never mean harm—they simply <strong>don’t know</strong>.<br>That’s exactly why I want to explain this clearly in this article.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Why Bras Are NG — A Gentle Explanation From a Professional</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Image_fx-5.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2887"/></figure>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Compression Lines Interfere With Diagnosis</strong></h3>

<p>Chest X-rays rely on very subtle shades of gray.<br>When bra lines appear, they can:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Mimic breast tissue shadows<br></li>

<li>Create horizontal lines near the diaphragm<br></li>

<li>Overlap with pulmonary vessels<br></li>

<li>Make radiologists wonder:<br><strong>“Is this pathology? Or just pressure marks?”</strong><strong><br></strong></li>
</ul>

<p>This confusion can delay diagnosis or trigger unnecessary additional tests.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Metal and Plastic Components Appear Clearly</strong></h3>

<p>Bra hooks, plastic rings on camisoles—<br>they all show up, unmistakably.</p>

<p>Sometimes these tiny beads or dots resemble calcifications.<br>Radiologists often react like:</p>

<p><strong>“Is this a lesion? Or a foreign body?”</strong></p>

<p>And it’s not just bras.<br>Necklaces, pendants, medicated patches, warmers—all of them show up.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Bra Tops Create the Worst Artifact: The “Staircase Shadow”</strong></h3>

<p>Even without metal, their compression is powerful:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>The body tissue forms steps<br></li>

<li>These steps appear as literal “stairs” on the X-ray<br></li>

<li>Radiologists double-check:<br><strong>“Is this just clothing? Or something abnormal?”</strong><strong><br></strong></li>
</ul>

<p>This is the true identity of the <strong>“mysterious staircase.”</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Camisoles and Their “Tiny Plastic Rings” — Also NG</strong></h3>

<p>The small circular adjusters on both straps?<br>Yes. They show up <em>very</em> clearly.</p>

<p>Most people think, “Something this small won’t appear.”<br>But X-rays are not forgiving.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Winter Brings the “Heat-Tech Problem”</strong></h3>

<p>Loose T-shirt-type layers → <strong>OK</strong><strong><br></strong> Tight-fitting, high-compression types → <strong>NG</strong><strong><br></strong> Tank-top types → <strong>Leaning NG</strong> (armpit compression is common)</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>So What Should You Wear?</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Image_fx-6.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2888"/></figure>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Correct Answer: ONE T-SHIRT</strong></h3>

<p>✔ Thin T-shirt<br>✔ Airism-type T-shirt<br>✔ Thin shirt with plastic buttons<br>✔ Loose heat-tech T-shirt style</p>

<p>As long as it meets:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>No metal<br></li>

<li>No plastic<br></li>

<li>No compression<br></li>
</ul>

<p>…you’re almost always good.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>❌ NG List (Save This!)</strong></h3>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><tbody><tr><td><strong>Item</strong></td><td><strong>Why It’s NG</strong></td></tr><tr><td>Bra</td><td>Metal, seams, compression</td></tr><tr><td>Bra top</td><td>Creates the “staircase” artifact</td></tr><tr><td>Camisole</td><td>Plastic rings appear clearly</td></tr><tr><td>Tank-top innerwear</td><td>Compression shadows around the armpit</td></tr><tr><td>Necklaces</td><td>Metal shadows</td></tr><tr><td>Medicated patches / warmers</td><td>Show up even on the back</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>How to Prepare on the Day (From a Tech’s Real Perspective)</strong></h2>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Wear your normal clothes to the venue<br></li>

<li>Put <strong>one T-shirt for imaging</strong> in your bag<br></li>

<li>In the changing area:<br><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Remove your bra<br></li>

<li>Put on the T-shirt<br></li>
</ul>
</li>

<li>Store your bra deep inside your bag for safety<br></li>

<li>After imaging, change back and you’re done<br></li>
</ul>

<p>Just remembering this makes everything perfect.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>A Retake Hurts </strong><strong><em>Everyone</em></strong></h2>

<p>A retake means:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>More time for you<br></li>

<li>Slightly increased radiation dose<br></li>

<li>Longer waiting time for people behind you<br></li>

<li>Workflow interruption for the staff<br></li>
</ul>

<p>By wearing appropriate clothing,<br><strong>you’re helping every single person visiting the clinic that day.</strong></p>

<p>That’s a wonderful form of quiet kindness.</p>
