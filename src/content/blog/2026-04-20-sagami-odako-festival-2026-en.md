<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="We_Went_for_a_Walk_After_Momijis_Rabies_Shot"></span>We Went for a Walk After Momiji&#8217;s Rabies Shot<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<p>That day, Momiji had her annual rabies vaccination.</p>
<p>After the shot, I wanted to take her somewhere she&#8217;d enjoy. Momiji loves the water, so I was torn between the river and the beach — until my wife settled the debate and we headed to the river.</p>
<p>We drove out to the Sagami River.</p>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/odako_3299-1.jpg" alt="Sagami River and Mount Oyama under a clear blue sky" /></figure>
<p>The sky was wide open and blue. Mount Oyama stood out sharp in the distance. A sunny day at the Sagami River is something else.</p>

<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="When_We_Got_to_the_River_Something_Was_Being_Built"></span>When We Got to the River, Something Was Being Built<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<p>When I climbed up to the levee and looked out over the riverbed, something was being built.</p>
<p>Large bamboo-framed structures were lying on the ground, some partially upright. &#8220;What is that?&#8221; My first thought: construction work. But it did not feel like that either.</p>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/odako_3291-1.jpg" alt="View of the giant kite construction site from the levee" /></figure>
<p>From the car, I could see about three of these structures being made in different spots across the wide riverbed.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator"/>
<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Up_Close_It_Was_Bigger_Than_I_Expected"></span>Up Close, It Was Bigger Than I Expected<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<p>When I got closer, just the frame blew me away. Bamboo poles woven into a grid, spreading wide across the ground. Each side was enormous. This is a kite? This thing goes up in the air?</p>
<p>Honestly, I had underestimated it. I knew about the Sagami Odako Festival, but I had basically thought &#8220;eh, it&#8217;s just a big kite.&#8221;</p>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/odako_3287-1.jpg" alt="Close-up of the bamboo frame of the giant kite" /></figure>
<p>When I looked it up later, the &#8220;Hachiken-dako&#8221; flown at the Shindo venue is <strong>14.5 meters on each side</strong> — the size of 128 tatami mats. It weighs around <strong>950 kg</strong>.</p>
<p>The tether rope is 3-4 cm thick and 200 meters long. It takes 80-100 people to fly it.</p>
<p>Yeah. Getting floored by just the frame makes complete sense. I was definitely underestimating this.</p>
<p>By the way — the &#8220;Japan&#8217;s largest&#8221; title refers specifically to the largest kite <strong>flown regularly every year</strong>. Not a one-time stunt. This scale has been sustained, year after year. That&#8217;s what makes it genuinely impressive.</p>

<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="A_Festival_Thats_Been_Going_for_Nearly_200_Years"></span>A Festival That&#8217;s Been Going for Nearly 200 Years<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/odako_history_design_v4.jpg" alt="Infographic: 200-year history of the Sagami Odako festival" width="750" height="1000"/></figure>
<p>Once that banner caught my eye, I had to look it up — and the history ran deeper than I imagined.</p>
<p>The Sagami no Odako Matsuri traces its origins to the <strong>late Edo period, the Tenpo era (1830-1844)</strong>.</p>
<p>It started as a tradition to celebrate the birth of a son — the first Boys&#8217; Day celebration. In farming communities of the time, the birth of an heir was a major event. Families would fly kites high into the sky to report the birth to the heavens — something close to a Shinto ritual.</p>
<p>Over time, from the mid-Meiji era onward, it became a community-wide festival. The kites grew bigger decade by decade. By the Showa era, it was one of the &#8220;Top 4 Tourism Events&#8221; in Sagamihara. In 2010, it was officially designated as an <strong>Intangible Folk Cultural Property of Sagamihara City</strong>.</p>
<p>One detail I love: every year, the festival has a pair of <strong>theme characters (daiji)</strong> painted on the giant kite — two kanji that reflect the spirit and hopes of that year.</p>

<p>Nearly 200 years, kept alive by the people of this community. No wonder just the frame leaves you speechless.</p>
<p>And this festival is not run by the city or a corporation. It&#8217;s driven by local <strong>volunteer preservation groups (Hozonkai)</strong> from four neighborhoods: Shindo, Kamiisobu, Shimoiisobu, and Katsusaka. Each group handles everything — building the kite, flying it on the day. It runs on passion, not a budget. That&#8217;s the heart of this festival.</p>

<p><strong>Can You Get There by Motorcycle?</strong></p>
<p>Technically yes, but I would not really recommend it. Temporary parking areas are unpaved and fill up fast. There are no designated motorcycle spots, and the ground can get muddy.</p>
<p>Honestly, I was thinking about riding my Super Cub to avoid the car traffic — but then I pictured getting my beloved Cub stuck in the mud. I&#8217;ll take the train.</p>
<p><strong>Crowd Size</strong></p>
<p>All four venues combined draw <strong>160,000-180,000 visitors</strong> every year. The all-time record was 180,000 in 2010. Even at the 2023 comeback after a four-year COVID break, 178,000 people showed up. One of the biggest Golden Week events in Kanagawa.</p>
<p>180,000 people. I was already shaken by just the frame — and I&#8217;ve been quietly postponing this festival year after year with my &#8220;maybe someday&#8221; attitude. Honestly, a bit embarrassing.</p>
<p><strong>Peak Crowd Times</strong></p>
<p>Expect the biggest crowds around <strong>2:00 PM</strong> on both days. For a more relaxed experience, aim for the <strong>morning</strong>.</p>
<p><strong>&#8220;Waiting for the Wind&#8221; Is Part of the Experience</strong></p>
<p>Flying a giant kite requires a steady wind of <strong>10-15 m/s</strong>. When it&#8217;s calm, everything goes into &#8220;wind waiting&#8221; mode — the kite stays on the ground. Visitors can get up close to the kite and even chat with the builders. The festival is not just the moment the kite flies.</p>
<p><strong>Rain Means Cancellation</strong></p>
<p>The washi paper surface is extremely sensitive to moisture. Rain makes it heavy and risks the whole structure collapsing — so <strong>the event is cancelled in rain</strong>. The call is made the morning of the event, announced via the official website and Sagamihara City Call Center (042-770-7777).</p>
