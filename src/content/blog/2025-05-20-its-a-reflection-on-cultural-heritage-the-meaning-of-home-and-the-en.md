<h3>🌍 A Perfect Day for Touring in Okutama</h3>

<p>It was one of those days where the ride just felt perfect.<br>The air was fresh, the scenery stunning. My Super Cub glided smoothly through the mountain curves.</p>

<p>After crossing the Minetani Bridge and riding alongside the lake, I saw a sign: “Yama no Furusato Mura.”<br>I turned in, just thinking I’d check it out.</p>

<h3>The Mysterious Dance at the Visitor Center</h3>

<p>While browsing the exhibition, I noticed a strange photo:<br>a man with a white-painted face, dressed in a bright red kimono, wearing a glittering crown.</p>

<p>“…Wait, that’s an old guy?”</p>

<p>At first, I honestly thought it was some eccentric hobby.<br>But the caption said it was taken in “Ogouchi,” and something about it being a local ritual.</p>

<p>Then I saw the name: <em>Kashima Odori</em>.</p>

<p>It wasn’t just some obscure local dance—<br>It was officially recognized by the Japanese government as a <strong>Cultural Property</strong>, and even registered by <strong>UNESCO</strong>.</p>

<p>What? How was something so important… just sitting here in this quiet mountain center?<br>It felt like a hidden gem being neglected.</p>

<p>No posters. No videos. Just a few photos on the wall.<br>They call it “a culture Japan can be proud of,” but honestly, it looked like no one was paying attention.</p>

<p>I’d never heard of this dance before.<br>When I think of UNESCO traditions, I think of big names—Awa Odori, Eisa, stuff you see on TV or in the news.</p>

<p>But <em>Kashima Odori</em>? It wasn’t flashy at all. No wild crowds. No loud drums.</p>

<p>And yet…<br>I found myself wanting to see it in person.</p>

<p>If it’s really world-class, I want to feel what that means—<br>not in headlines, but in the quiet heartbeat of the people who dance it.</p>

<p>You can feel the quiet power and gentle pride of the tradition in every movement.<br>It’s not flashy—but that’s exactly what makes it special.</p>

<h3>Why Are These Old Guys Dressed as Women?</h3>

<p>I couldn’t shake the image, so I did some digging online.</p>

<p>Turns out, <em>Kashima Odori</em> has always been performed by men dressed as women.<br>That wasn’t just a quirky modern twist—it’s tradition.<br>The style goes all the way back to the Edo period and the world of kabuki.</p>

<p>Back then, women weren’t allowed on stage.<br>So boys and young men played female roles.<br>That theatrical style trickled down into regional festivals and somehow made its way to this quiet mountain village.</p>

<p>Still, I’ll be honest—seeing older guys in red kimono, their faces painted white, moving in slow motion… it looked kind of awkward.<br>No loud drums. No wild cheering.<br>Not exactly the kind of vibe you’d expect from something UNESCO-listed.</p>

<p>But maybe that’s exactly why it stuck with me.</p>

<p>It wasn’t flashy. It wasn’t polished.<br>But there was something about it that made me want to understand more.</p>

<p>Sometimes, the quietest things echo the loudest—<br>you just have to lean in close enough to hear them.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/3e9444df9895d5e3d6e9bac063f26326.png" alt="" class="wp-image-886"/></figure>

<h3>A Tradition Dating Back to the Edo Period</h3>

<p>Digging deeper, I found that <em>Kashima Odori</em> goes all the way back to the early Edo period.<br>It was originally called <em>Gion Odori</em>, and over time, one line in the lyrics—“Let us dance the Kashima Odori”—gave the tradition its new name.</p>

<p>When I read “Gion Odori,” I froze.<br>Gion? Like, <strong>Kyoto</strong>’s Gion?<br>That’s my hometown.<br>I never imagined I’d hear that name up in the mountains of Okutama.</p>

<p>Some believe the dance was passed down by noble families who fled Kyoto.<br>Others say traveling monks spread it as they wandered across the country.<br>We may never know the full story.</p>

<p>But just the thought that a fragment of Kyoto’s ancient spirit survived here—quietly, tucked into this remote village—moved me.</p>

<p>The performance begins with <em>Sanbasō</em>, a ritual dance praying for a bountiful harvest.<br>It’s not just a show—it’s a greeting to the gods. A sign of respect before the celebration begins.</p>

<p>The final act, called <em>Sanbyōshi</em>, is more playful and flamboyant.<br>They spin in circles, whip their kimono sleeves through the air—it feels like a throwback to the old-school <em>kabuki</em> rebels.</p>

<p>Start with reverence. End with joy.<br>That’s the rhythm of the dance.<br>And it beautifully captures what a true village festival is all about.</p>

<h3>The Sunken Village and the People Who Still Dance</h3>

<p>And then came the part that truly shook me.</p>

<p>The village where <em>Kashima Odori</em> was born—Ogouchi—<br><strong>no longer exists on any map</strong>.</p>

<p>In the 1950s, it was submerged to make way for the construction of Ogouchi Dam.<br>The entire village—its homes, schools, shrines—was swallowed by the rising waters.</p>

<p>I felt a lump in my throat.</p>

<p>A place that survived for centuries… wiped out in the name of progress.<br>And yet, somehow, the dance remained.</p>

<p>Even though the village disappeared, the people didn’t forget.<br>Those who grew up there—or their children, or grandchildren—<br>they still gather in the mountains every September.</p>

<p>They come to perform the dance.<br>They come to remember.</p>

<p>They come to a place called Ogouchi Shrine, built near the lake’s edge.<br>It combines the spirits of the nine original shrines that once stood in the sunken village.</p>

<p>And that’s when it hit me:</p>

<p>These people don’t just return home.<br><strong>They return through the dance</strong>.</p>

<p>It’s not just a tradition—it’s a reason.<br>A reason to come back.<br>A reason to stay connected.<br>A reason to keep something alive that water couldn’t drown.</p>

<p>That…<br>is power.</p>

<h3>How Dance Keeps the Hometown Alive</h3>

<p>Why was it the <strong>dance</strong> that survived?<br>Why not the buildings, or the roads, or the shrines?</p>

<p>The village was gone—sunken beneath a lake.<br>Its schools, its houses, its sacred places—all erased from sight.</p>

<p>And yet, the dance remained.<br>The people remained.<br>And through that dance… the village still breathes.</p>

<p>That’s when I realized something:<br><strong>Tangible things vanish. But spirit endures.</strong></p>

<p>And every year, <em>Kashima Odori</em> brings that spirit back to life.</p>

<p>People return—not to a place, but to a feeling.<br>A rhythm. A memory.<br>A shared promise that this place, and its soul, still matter.</p>

<p>You can lose your home.<br>You can lose your school.<br>You can even lose your parents.</p>

<p>But if there's something to come back to—even just a dance—<br>then you still have a way to find your roots.</p>

<p><em>Kashima Odori</em> isn’t just a performance.<br>It’s a doorway.<br>A bridge that lets people return—<br>to where they came from,<br>to who they are.</p>

<h3>Just a Random Stop, But──</h3>

<p>Honestly, I had no plan.<br>I was just cruising on my Cub, enjoying the breeze, when I thought,<br>"Hey, there's a visitor center. Might as well take a peek."</p>

<p>That’s all it was. A pit stop.</p>

<p>But I didn’t expect it to stir so much in me.</p>

<p>What started as a laugh at men in face paint and red kimono<br>soon turned into a full-on research spiral.<br>I watched videos. I read articles.<br>And before I knew it, I was lost in a story I never knew existed.</p>

<p>A sunken village.<br>A dance that survived.<br>People who return, year after year, just to move in rhythm with their memories.</p>

<p>All from one random stop.</p>

<p>I left that center with the same wind on my face…<br>but something in the way I looked at the world had shifted.<br>Just a little.</p>

<p>Sometimes, you don’t need a grand plan.<br>You just need to <strong>pause in the right place</strong>—<br>and let the story find you.</p>

<h3>Sometimes, World-Class Culture Hides in the Hills</h3>

<p>When I hear “UNESCO-registered” or “Intangible Cultural Heritage,”<br>I picture something grand—<br>Kyoto’s Gion Festival, the Great Buddha of Nara…<br>major landmarks, spotlighted in guidebooks and glossy posters.</p>

<p>But the truly powerful things?</p>

<p>They often live quietly.<br>Hidden in the folds of the hills.<br>Whispered through tradition, not shouted from rooftops.</p>

<p>This one lived in the mountains of Okutama.<br>By a lake.<br>In a place where people once prayed, played, and built their lives.<br>A village now sunken beneath water.</p>

<p>But the dance remained.<br>And so did the people who kept it alive.</p>

<p>Every year, they return—not for a show, but for something deeper.</p>

<p>Even though the village is gone, its spirit breathes on.</p>

<p>You can’t see it in stone or wood.<br>But if you listen close, you can feel it in the rhythm.<br>In the footsteps.<br>In the silence between the beats.</p>

<p>And then I wondered…</p>

<p><strong>Where is that place for me?</strong></p>

<p>Maybe it’s that overgrown riverbank from my childhood,<br>where we played out Gundam battles with sticks and shouts.<br>Just a patch of grass to others—<br>but to us, it was everything.</p>

<p>We all carry places like that.<br>Tiny worlds we never really leave.</p>

<h3>🌍 Final Thoughts: When Culture Quietly Moves the Heart</h3>

<p>I stopped by without a plan.<br>And found the spirit of a village that has lived for over 400 years.</p>

<p>UNESCO heritage isn’t always loud or famous.<br>Sometimes, it hides in quiet places—<br>in ordinary scenery,<br>in rituals nearly forgotten.</p>

<p>Finding a tradition no one talks about...<br>somehow made me remember the little corners of my own past.<br>The places and feelings I thought I’d outgrown.</p>

<p>That was the day I met <em>Kashima Odori</em>.<br>And maybe…<br>it was also the day I re-met a small part of myself.</p>
