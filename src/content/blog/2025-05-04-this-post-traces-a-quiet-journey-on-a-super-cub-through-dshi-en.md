<h4>"Where does the city's water come from?"</h4>

<p>Have you ever stopped to think about that?</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/PXL_20250503_233958318-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-361"/></figure>

<p>During Japan’s Golden Week of 2025, I set out on my Super Cub from Sagamihara in Kanagawa Prefecture, aiming to reach Lake Yamanaka by passing through Dōshi Village in Yamanashi.<br>The lush, winding Route 413—known as Dōshi-michi—was more than just a scenic ride.<br>It turns out this deep forested area in Dōshi is the actual water source for the city of Yokohama.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h4>Dōshi Road: Hidden Nature Behind the Urban Curtain</h4>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/PXL_20250504_000324372-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-354"/></figure>

<p>Dōshi Village lies at the southeastern edge of Yamanashi Prefecture, right along the border with Kanagawa.<br>It's a quiet mountain village, far removed from the bustle of urban life.<br>You wouldn’t guess it just walking through Yokohama, but this place quietly sustains the city’s water supply.<br>The Dōshi River runs through the village center and has been providing fresh water to Yokohama since the Meiji era.</p>

<div class="wp-block-columns"><div class="wp-block-column"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/PXL_20250503_235107698-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-352"/></figure>
</div>

<div class="wp-block-column"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/PXL_20250504_002149079.MP-1-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-357"/></figure>
</div>
</div>

<p>So pure it's said to “stay fresh even beyond the equator,” Dōshi’s water is more than legend.<br>Roughly 30% of the village is protected as a water conservation forest by Yokohama City, preserving this natural lifeline.</p>

<p>As I rode past on my bike, a small stone marker and sign came into view—<br>a quiet reminder of the invisible bond between this forest and the distant city.</p>

<h4>Mt. Fuji and Lake Yamanaka: The Source of Sky and Water</h4>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/PXL_20250504_010142294-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-360"/></figure>

<p>After cruising along Dōshi-michi, I arrived at the expansive Lake Yamanaka—<br>one of the five lakes surrounding Mt. Fuji, nestled quietly on its northeastern base.</p>

<p>I parked my bike by the lakeside and looked up—<br>there it was, Mt. Fuji in all its clarity beneath a flawless sky.<br>The cool breeze and crisp air held me still in awe.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/PXL_20250504_014323829-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-359"/></figure>

<p>More than just a sightseeing spot, Mt. Fuji felt like a spiritual thread<br>connecting Dōshi, Lake Yamanaka, and the distant city of Yokohama.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h4>Fujino Hot Springs and the Road Back Home</h4>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/PXL_20250504_021924101-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-353"/></figure>

<p>To be honest, my back had been aching that morning, and I wasn’t really in the mood to ride.</p>

<p>But after stepping out for an early morning bowl of ramen, I casually hopped on my Super Cub.<br>Next thing I knew, I had crossed Dōshi and made it all the way to Lake Yamanaka.</p>

<p>I stopped by Fujino’s Yamanami Onsen, but the crowd made me turn back.<br>Even so, the cool forest air and the glimpse of Mt. Fuji brought a quiet comfort to my soul.</p>

<p>When I got home and cracked open a beer, I thought to myself—</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h4>The Invisible Path of Water That Connects Us</h4>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/PXL_20250503_235030880-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-362"/></figure>

<p>Just like how Kyoto's water flows quietly from Lake Biwa and the Uji River in Shiga,<br>I realized that Yokohama's water too travels silently from the forest of Dōshi.</p>

<p>The water flowing beneath Yokohama’s towers—<br>it began deep in the Dōshi forest, followed the mountain paths near Lake Yamanaka, and made its gentle journey to the city as a “drop of the forest.”</p>

<p>Things I learned only by traveling—<br>connections you won’t find on any map,<br>a hidden relationship between urban life and mountain nature.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p>Your “just because” kind of day might lead to something unexpectedly meaningful.<br>Take a slow ride, and let Dōshi’s forest remind you of the quiet connections that shape our lives.</p>
