<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>English Note:</strong></p>

<p>During Japan's post-war economic boom, the country embraced a spirit of innovation and investment. From bullet trains to electronics, Japan was often the first to do what no one else had dared to try. This boldness helped shape a time when the world admired Japan’s forward-thinking mindset.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>English Note:</strong></p>

<p>Since the burst of the economic bubble, Japan has become more cautious—perhaps too cautious. Innovation is stalled by fear of failure, and bold decisions are often postponed until others succeed first. This risk-averse culture has contributed to what’s now called “The Lost 30 Years.</p>

<p>The case of Winny, a peer-to-peer file-sharing software in Japan, offers a powerful lesson.<br>Though the software itself was neutral, its creator Isamu Kaneko was arrested when others used it illegally.<br>He was later found not guilty—but the damage to trust, innovation, and hope was already done.</p>

<p>It reminds us that technology isn’t the problem.<br>How we respond to it, and how society chooses to treat creators, makes all the difference.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>English Note:</strong></p>

<p>Technology is evolving fast. But are we, the people, evolving with it? In Japan, overly strict regulations may be stifling the seeds of future progress. With aging populations and labor shortages, we need AI—not to replace us, but to help us. The key is knowing how to use it wisely.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>English Note:</strong></p>

<p>This blog may seem like just my personal thoughts, but if even one reader feels a little more aware or protected after reading, it’s worth it. If fewer people are scammed, if someone’s trust or savings are kept safe—then this blog truly serves a purpose.</p>

<p><strong>English Summary:</strong></p>

<p>What AI romance scams really reveal is something deeper: our human vulnerability—the desire to believe, to connect.</p>

<p>That’s why we must not only protect ourselves, but also choose trust wisely.</p>

<p>So here I am, thinking together with Chappy.<br>Not fearing AI, but learning how to live alongside it.<br>And taking small steps, hoping Japan becomes a country of bold challenges once again.</p>
