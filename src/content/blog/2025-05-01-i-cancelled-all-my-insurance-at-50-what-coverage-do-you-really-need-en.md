<h3 id="a-news-headline-that-hit-close-to-home" class="wp-block-heading">A News Headline That Hit Close to Home</h3>

<p>Not long ago, a headline stopped me cold: "510,000 Personal Records Possibly Leaked from Insurance Broker."<br>The company in question was a well-known insurance consulting firm.</p>

<p>Honestly, I'd never used that particular firm — but the timing felt strangely personal.<br>Just recently, a YouTube video had nudged me to finally review every single insurance policy I was holding. And I'd just finished cancelling them all.</p>

<p>"Maybe I really did make the right call," I thought.</p>

<p>A quiet wave of relief washed over me.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 id="the-insurance-heavy-years" class="wp-block-heading">The Insurance-Heavy Years</h3>

<p>When my two kids were small, I was covered for just about everything. Back then, it seemed like the responsible thing to do:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Life insurance (to protect the family if something happened to me)</li>
<li>Medical insurance (for hospitalization and surgery)</li>
<li>Cancer insurance (just in case)</li>
<li>Personal pension insurance (for retirement savings)</li>
</ul>

<p>At the time, the monthly premiums added up to a pretty significant chunk of our household budget.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 id="the-kids-are-out-on-their-own-time-to-re" class="wp-block-heading">The Kids Are Out on Their Own. Time to Reassess.</h3>

<p>Both boys are now working adults. With just my wife and me in the house, I finally sat down and asked myself: "Do I really still need all of this?"</p>

<p>After taking a hard look, here's what I decided:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Medical and cancer insurance: Cancelled</strong><br>→ Japan's high-cost medical care system (高額療養費制度) caps your out-of-pocket costs each month. Combined with employer sick pay, public coverage turned out to be more than enough.</li>

<li><strong>Life insurance: Cancelled</strong><br>→ With the kids grown and independent, no one truly relies on my income anymore. If something happened, our savings would cover a proper funeral.</li>

<li><strong>Personal pension insurance: Already cancelled</strong> (I took a small hit on the surrender value, but I was at peace with that.)</li>
</ul>

<p>What I kept:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Home fire insurance</strong> (we own our house, so this one's non-negotiable)</li>
<li><strong>Car insurance</strong> (absolutely essential)</li>
</ul>

<p>And the result? <strong>Our monthly fixed costs dropped dramatically. And honestly, a weight I hadn't even noticed lifted from my shoulders.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 id="from-personal-pension-to-index-funds" class="wp-block-heading">From Personal Pension to Index Funds</h3>

<p>My old personal pension plan was set to pay out from age 65 — but when I actually ran the numbers on the management fees and projected returns, the picture wasn't pretty.<br>A low-cost index fund would almost certainly outperform it over the same period.</p>

<p>So I made the switch.</p>

<p>Now I'm building my own retirement savings, on my own schedule, with my own risk tolerance.<br>I used to think that was something only financial experts did. Now I think it's just what this era requires.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 id="savings-beat-insurance-for-medical-costs" class="wp-block-heading">Savings Beat Insurance for Medical Costs</h3>

<p>Medical insurance sounds reassuring. But when I thought it through carefully:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Daily hospitalization payouts are often not enough to cover real costs</li>
<li>Hospital stays have gotten shorter — many procedures are now outpatient</li>
<li>The payout conditions are surprisingly complicated</li>
</ul>

<p>So I asked myself: "Why not just save that monthly premium instead?"</p>

<p>In Japan, <strong>the high-cost medical care system</strong> limits what you actually pay out of pocket for medical expenses in a given month. For most people in their 50s, that government safety net covers the big stuff.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 id="coverage-you-choose--not-coverage-you-in" class="wp-block-heading">Coverage You Choose — Not Coverage You Inherited</h3>

<p>I'm not saying everyone should cancel everything. But I do believe there's a real difference between intentionally choosing coverage and just keeping policies because "I've always had them."</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>"Does this still actually fit who I am today?"</p>
</blockquote>

<p>That's the question worth sitting with.</p>

<p>For our household, the answer led to:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Meaningfully lower monthly fixed costs</li>
<li>More money flowing toward things we actually value</li>
<li>A simpler, calmer financial life</li>
</ul>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 id="the-day-a-news-story-confirmed-my-choice" class="wp-block-heading">The Day a News Story Confirmed My Choice</h3>

<p>When I saw that data breach headline, it felt strangely validating — not because anything bad had happened to me, but because it served as a quiet reminder: decisions we make thoughtfully tend to hold up over time.</p>

<p>If you're in your 50s, with your kids out of the house and your financial responsibilities shifting — this might be exactly the right moment to take a fresh, honest look at your coverage.</p>
