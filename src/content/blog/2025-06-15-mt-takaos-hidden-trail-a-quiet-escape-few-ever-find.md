<p><strong>🌲 Introduction</strong></p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>Mount Takao is one of Japan’s most visited mountains, known for its easy access and vibrant crowds.<br>But hidden just beyond the popular trails lies a quieter world — a secret side of Takao that few travelers ever see.</p>

<p>This time, I explored that hidden path.<br>Starting from the Hikagezawa parking lot with my Shiba Inu, Momiji, I originally planned to hike via the main trail.<br>But a fallen tree blocked the way, and I had to change my route.</p>

<p>What I found instead was something unexpected and unforgettable:<br>a peaceful walk through whispering forests, the sound of streams at my side, and not a single tourist in sight.<br>Just nature, silence, and a slow-paced adventure — the kind that stays with you long after the hike ends.</p>
</blockquote>

<h2 id="chapter-1-think-takao-is-just-crowds-and" class="wp-block-heading">🏞️ Chapter 1: Think Takao Is Just Crowds and Tourists? Think Again.</h2>

<div class="wp-block-columns"><div class="wp-block-column" style="flex-basis:66.66%"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_005246010-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1738"/></figure>
</div>

<div class="wp-block-column" style="flex-basis:33.33%"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_032804256.MP_-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1737"/></figure>
</div>
</div>

<p>When people hear “Mount Takao,” they usually imagine bustling walkways, souvenir shops, and long lines at cable cars.<br>That’s the well-known front side — the Omotesando Route — loved by tourists from all over the world.</p>

<p>But on the opposite side of the mountain, there's a trail that feels like an entirely different world.<br>It’s called the “Ura-Takao Route” — the Hidden Takao Trail.<br>Fewer crowds, deeper forests, and a calm that stretches on like time has stopped.</p>

<p>On that day, I planned to hike from the Hikagezawa parking lot through Mount Takao to Kobotoke-Shiroyama.<br>But a sign at the trailhead warned: “Trail Closed Due to Fallen Trees.”<br>So I changed plans and headed straight to Kobotoke-Shiroyama — and honestly, it might have been the better choice.</p>

<p>The path was well-maintained, easy to walk, yet filled with the sounds of rustling leaves, birdsong, and flowing streams.<br>There were even a few moments where the trail felt more like a hidden shrine path than a hiking route.</p>

<p>Skipping the main summit led me to something even more special:<br>a peaceful, uncrowded journey through what felt like Takao’s true soul.</p>

<h2 id="chapter-2-a-creekside-welcome--nature-ta" class="wp-block-heading">🌿 Chapter 2: A Creekside Welcome — Nature Takes Over From the First Step</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_010352998-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1728"/></figure>

<p>The moment we stepped out of the car at Hikagezawa Parking Lot, the soundscape changed.<br>No cars. No city noise. Just the soft murmur of a creek, birds chirping, and leaves swaying in the wind.</p>

<p>It felt like nature wrapped around us instantly — like the forest had been waiting.</p>

<p>And then there’s my Shiba Inu, Momiji.<br>The second she spotted the water, she lit up.<br>Sniffing around, wagging her tail like a windmill, dipping her nose toward the stream… and then — splash!<br>She dashed into the water like it was her own private theme park.<br>I couldn’t help but laugh. She was fully in the moment, fully alive.</p>

<p>We walked deeper in, and even the phone signal began to fade.<br>No notifications, no messages — just footsteps and forest.</p>

<p>The path was mostly shaded, and the air?<br>Cooler than expected for June — almost chilly in places.<br>It made me wonder, “Wait, is this really summer?”</p>

<p>Unlike the popular routes, this one didn’t shout for attention.<br>It whispered.<br>And in that whisper, the mountain gave us a gentle invitation —<br>to slow down, breathe deeply, and just <em>be</em>.</p>

<h2 id="chapter-3-a-quiet-forest-road-going-up-a" class="wp-block-heading">🟩 Chapter 3: A Quiet Forest Road Going Up, and a Taste of Real Hiking Coming Down</h2>

<div class="wp-block-columns"><div class="wp-block-column" style="flex-basis:33.33%"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_020320759-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1729"/></figure>
</div>

<div class="wp-block-column" style="flex-basis:66.66%"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_012831546-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1730"/></figure>
</div>
</div>

<p>The uphill path we chose was a paved forest road — wide, well-kept, and completely calm.<br>We passed just one work truck. Aside from that, it was only us, trees, birdsong, and the wind.</p>

<p>It wasn’t a hike.<br>It was a peaceful stroll — or maybe more like a slow dog walk in the forest.<br>Momiji led the way, ears perked, nose twitching, tail swaying like a pendulum.<br>She seemed to love the calmness as much as I did.</p>

<p>Then, surprisingly, a group of trail runners came flying by.<br>I thought, “Wait, people run here?”<br>We smiled, nodded, and let them pass, then returned to our pace — slow, steady, and soft.</p>

<p>For the return trip, we switched things up and took a dirt trail down.<br>It was narrower, a bit more rugged, and it gave us that “real hiking” feeling.<br>But the slope wasn’t too steep, and the trail was easy to follow — even for a casual hiker.</p>

<p>Climbing up on the paved path felt safe and gentle.<br>Coming down on the mountain trail brought us closer to nature.<br>Being able to balance both sides of the mountain — that’s the hidden beauty of the Ura-Takao route.</p>

<h2 id="chapter-4-at-the-top-of-mt-kobotoke-shir" class="wp-block-heading">🌳 Chapter 4: At the Top of Mt. Kobotoke-Shiroyama — Silence, Sky, and a Bit of Surprise</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_022627647-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1731"/></figure>

<p>We made it to the summit of Mt. Kobotoke-Shiroyama!<br>Benches and tables were scattered around, offering a nice little rest area.<br>It was a weekday, so things were quiet, but a few other hikers were enjoying the peaceful vibes.</p>

<p>Then we saw it — a small teahouse near the summit… and a mysterious <strong>tengu</strong> statue staring at us.<br>Its eyes locked onto mine, and for a second, I froze.<br>Creepy? A bit. But charming in its own quirky, mountain-spirit way.</p>

<p>On weekends, the teahouse serves warm dishes like miso soup and cup noodles, but today it was closed.<br>Honestly? That made the place feel even more serene.<br>The sky felt wider. The air, lighter.</p>

<p>Momiji, who had been bursting with energy earlier, flopped down and sprawled out beside me.<br>Total Zen mode.<br>It’s funny — watching her relax made me relax even more.</p>

<p>I sat on a bench, let the wind brush against my face, and simply breathed.<br>That small moment carried a quiet joy — the kind you don’t get in crowded places.</p>

<p>And you know what?<br>After all that walking, I <em>felt</em> like I’d burned some calories.<br>Did I actually lose weight? Probably not.<br>But hey — for a 50-something guy like me, just feeling that “I moved today” was enough.</p>

<h2 id="chapter-5-not-the-tourist-trail--real-st" class="wp-block-heading">🟩 Chapter 5: Not the Tourist Trail — Real Stillness Lives in the "Back" of Mt. Takao</h2>

<div class="wp-block-columns"><div class="wp-block-column" style="flex-basis:33.33%"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_010344520-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1736"/></figure>

<div class="wp-block-column" style="flex-basis:66.66%"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_010240121-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1733"/></figure>
</div>
</div>

<p>After resting at the summit teahouse, we headed back down — this time via the mountain trail.<br>Unlike the paved road we climbed, the descent was framed by trees, soft earth, and birdsong.</p>

<p>The smell of soil.<br>The rustle of leaves overhead.<br>The cool breeze brushing against our legs.<br>There were moments when we didn’t see another soul — and it felt like the forest had stopped time just for us.</p>

<p>Because it was a weekday, the tea shop was closed.<br>But honestly, just sitting side by side with Momiji, feeling the wind and doing nothing, was more than enough.</p>

<p>That’s when it really hit me:<br>The “Back of Takao” isn’t just a quieter path — it’s a different mountain entirely.<br>There’s no dramatic view, no cheerful crowd.<br>Instead, there’s something else: a kind of presence, a whisper of the mountain’s deeper self.</p>

<p>They say Mt. Takao was once called <strong>Reiki Manzan</strong> — “a mountain full of sacred energy.”<br>And here, in the silence of its lesser-known trails, I finally understood why.<br>It wasn’t just a walk. It felt like stepping into something ancient and still.</p>

<h2 id="chapter-6-how-to-find-the-quiet-side--ge" class="wp-block-heading">🚌🚗 Chapter 6: How to Find the Quiet Side — Getting to Hikagezawa Parking Area</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/PXL_20250613_003819009-scaled.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-1725"/></figure>

<p>Our starting point was <strong>Hikagezawa Parking Area</strong>, the gateway to the quiet “Back of Takao.”<br>It’s a spot far removed from the crowded main trails — perfect for those seeking a peaceful nature walk.</p>

<p>Here’s how to get there, whether you're driving or using public transportation:</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">🚗 By Car:</h3>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>From the <strong>Ken-Ō Expressway (圏央道)</strong>, take the <strong>Takao IC Exit</strong> and head toward Shinjuku via Route 20.</li>

<li>Turn left at the <strong>Nishi-Asakawa intersection</strong>, then drive about 3 km along Route 516 (Asakawa-Sagamiko Line).</li>

<li>Watch carefully for a <strong>small forest road on the left</strong> — it’s easy to miss!</li>

<li>Enter the forest road and the free <strong>Hikagezawa Parking Area (about 10 spaces)</strong> will be immediately on your right.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>🟡 <em>Note: There are no toilets at the parking area, but you can use the facilities at Hikagezawa Campground, about a 10-minute walk away.</em><br>🟡 <em>Search “日影沢駐車場” (Hikagezawa Parking) on Google Maps to navigate easily.</em></p>

<p>📍 <strong>Google Maps</strong>: <a class="" href="https://www.google.com/maps/place/%E6%97%A5%E5%BD%B1%E6%B2%A2%E9%A7%90%E8%BB%8A%E5%A0%B4/@35.638365,139.2398431,17z/data=!4m6!3m5!1s0x6019193ab80427a5:0x1f27a0b9226cce6!8m2!3d35.638365!4d139.242418!16s%2Fg%2F11ry5mg_v1?entry=ttu&amp;g_ep=EgoyMDI1MDYxMS4wIKXMDSoASAFQAw%3D%3D">Hikagezawa Parking Lot</a> (direct link)</p>
</blockquote>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">🚌 By Train + Bus (Recommended):</h3>

<ol class="wp-block-list"><li>Take the <strong>JR Chūō Line</strong> to <strong>Takao Station</strong>, not Takao-sanguchi!</li>

<li>Exit from the <strong>North Exit</strong>, and board the <strong>Keiō Bus bound for Kobotoke (小仏行き)</strong><br><em>(Buses come 1–2 times per hour)</em></li>

<li>Get off at the <strong>Hikage (日影) Bus Stop</strong>.</li>

<li>From there, it’s just a <strong>3-minute walk</strong> to the parking area — signs will guide you.</li>
</ol>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>🟠 <em>Tip: Cell reception may be limited along parts of the trail, so download maps in advance!</em></p>
</blockquote>
