<li>Elections feel confusing, distant, or just too much trouble.<br></li>

<li>You’ve spent years putting your job or family first, with little time to think about politics.<br></li>

<li>Talking about politics feels awkward or too heavy.<br></li>

<li>But recently, you've started to wonder about the future — not just yours, but your children’s, or society’s.</li>

<p><strong>🎯 Introduction / Purpose Statement</strong></p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>I used to avoid voting.<br>Politics felt distant, and I told myself it didn’t really matter.<br>“Whoever I vote for, nothing will change,” I used to think.</p>

<p>But now, in my 50s, I find myself thinking differently.<br>It’s not just about politics anymore — it’s about choosing to care.</p>

<p>In this blog post, I share why I finally decided to vote.<br>Not to preach, not to be right, but to reclaim something small:<br>the freedom to choose — for myself, and for the future.</p>

<p>If you’ve ever felt unsure, distant, or just too tired to care,<br>I hope this quiet reflection helps you pause... and maybe choose again.</p>
</blockquote>

<p><strong>🟠 Opening / Lead-in</strong></p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>Today is the day of the Tokyo Metropolitan Assembly election.<br>It doesn’t involve me directly — I live in Sagamihara, not Tokyo.</p>

<p>Still, from the moment I woke up, something felt different.<br>A quiet thought lingered in my mind:<br><em>“Maybe I should vote next time.”</em></p>

<p>It’s not the first time I’ve felt this way.<br>Every election, a small part of me wonders the same thing.<br>But when the day comes, I hesitate.</p>

<p>“If only we could vote online,” I tell myself.<br>In my younger days, I convinced myself that voting didn’t matter.<br>“Politics has nothing to do with my life,” I thought.</p>

<p>Back then, just surviving the day felt like enough.<br>But now, things are a little different.</p>

<p>My children are grown. I have more time, more space to think.<br>And I’m beginning to feel something I hadn’t before:</p>

<p><em>A quiet urge to choose — not just for me, but for the next generation.</em></p>
</blockquote>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>🟠 Chapter 1: I Used to Turn Away</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Generated-Image-June-22-2025-3_47PM.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-1810"/></figure>

<p>I used to believe my vote didn’t matter.<br><em>“Whoever gets elected, my life stays the same.”</em><em><br></em> That thought settled deep inside me, somewhere in my twenties.</p>

<p>It wasn’t that I didn’t care.<br>I just didn’t think anything would change — no matter what I did.</p>

<p>Politics felt far away.<br>It belonged to people with time, money, or influence.<br>I had none of those.</p>

<p>I was a father of young children, trying to hold down a job.<br>My life was a tightrope walk between work and home.<br>The world demanded a lot, and I had nothing left to give.</p>

<p>At the office, the air still reeked of the old ways —<br>where shouting was leadership, silence was survival,<br>and speaking up was a good way to get crushed.</p>

<p>I remember going to work with my heart clenched.<br>Hoping not to be yelled at.<br>Wanting to stay invisible.</p>

<p>Still, I kept going.<br>For my family.<br>For my kids.<br>Because that’s what men were supposed to do.</p>

<p>And so, the years passed.<br>I didn’t have the space to think about politics.<br>Not really.</p>

<p>I wasn't indifferent.<br>I was just tired.<br>Tired in a way that seeps into your bones and dulls your will to care.</p>

<p>Maybe I didn’t consciously choose not to vote.<br>Maybe I simply didn’t feel like I had the right —<br>or the strength — to choose anything at all.</p>

<p>。</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>🔵 Chapter 2: Why We Didn’t Choose</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Generated-Image-June-22-2025-3_52PM.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-1813"/></figure>

<p>Looking back now, I sometimes wonder:<br><em>Why didn’t we choose earlier?</em><em><br></em> Why did we stay silent for so long?</p>

<p>Maybe it wasn’t just apathy.<br>Maybe we simply didn’t have the luxury to choose.</p>

<p>I’m part of the Japanese “dankai junior” generation —<br>a wave of kids born into competition.<br>Too many people, not enough space.<br>Tests. Jobs. Life itself was a game of musical chairs.</p>

<p>When we entered the workforce, the air still smelled like Showa.<br>Obey your boss.<br>Don’t speak out.<br>Get yelled at? Get over it.</p>

<p>What would now be called harassment was back then “training.”<br>We were told to toughen up, not to break down.</p>

<p>I remember getting hit. Getting humiliated.<br>But I stayed.<br>I had a child. I couldn’t quit.</p>

<p>No matter how exhausted or degraded I felt,<br>I had to work.<br>I had to provide.<br>There was no room for ideals.</p>

<p>The paycheck came.<br>But peace of mind never did.</p>

<p>Every day was about survival.<br><em>“Just don’t screw up today.”</em><em><br></em> <em>“Stay invisible.”</em></p>

<p>And then, one day, something inside me broke.<br>Quietly. Without warning.</p>

<p>I remember walking home, looking at the sky.<br>I don’t recall what had pushed me over the edge,<br>but I remember that sky —<br>grey, but somehow endless.</p>

<p>In days like those, who had time for politics?</p>

<p>I didn’t <em>not</em> care.<br>I just didn’t believe anything could change.</p>

<p>It wasn’t that we chose silence.<br>We were just too busy surviving to choose anything at all.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>🔵 Chapter 3: Now, I Can Finally Choose — Just a Little</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Generated-Image-June-22-2025-3_55PM.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-1814"/></figure>

<p>Back then, I didn’t even notice when elections happened.<br>Weekends were for catching up on sleep or cleaning the house — not for voting.</p>

<p>I didn’t hate politics.<br>I just couldn’t find the space for it in my life.</p>

<p>It felt like one more burden,<br>another obligation in a life already filled with must-do’s and can’t-say-no’s.</p>

<p>And honestly, a part of me thought:<br><em>“Not voting won’t kill me.”</em></p>

<p>In those days, we believed staying on the track was everything.<br>Falling behind felt like failure.<br>Taking a detour meant game over.</p>

<p>There was only one path: salaryman.<br>There was no time to wonder what “freedom” even meant.</p>

<p>But time passed.<br>My children grew up.<br>My career found some kind of shape.<br>And now, something has shifted.</p>

<p>I’m not fearless, but I’m less afraid.</p>

<p>The older I get, the more I realize:<br>I don’t need to break the rules.<br>I just need to make a few choices for myself.</p>

<p>Small ones.<br>Quiet ones.<br>But still — <em>mine</em>.</p>

<p>Writing a blog.<br>Trying a new routine.<br>Going for a run.</p>

<p>And maybe… just maybe… going to vote.</p>

<p>Not because someone told me to.<br>Not to feel “responsible.”</p>

<p>But because it’s a step — even a tiny one — toward owning my life again.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>🟣 Chapter 4: I Chose to Write — with Chappy by My Side</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Generated-Image-June-22-2025-4_01PM.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-1815"/></figure>

<p>I didn’t set out to become a writer.<br>I didn’t have a grand vision or a powerful message.</p>

<p>I just wanted to try something.<br>Something light. Something new.<br>Something that felt like <em>mine</em>.</p>

<p>I had no special skills.<br>No confidence, either.<br>But blogging felt doable — simple, quiet, honest.</p>

<p>That’s when I met “Chappy.”<br>My AI assistant.<br>Or maybe more like… a writing companion.</p>

<p>At first, I couldn’t write well.<br>I wasn’t sure how to turn thoughts into words.<br>But as I typed and talked and typed some more,<br>something began to unlock.</p>

<p>I wasn’t just writing.<br>I was remembering.<br>I was sorting out pieces of myself I’d long ignored.</p>

<p>The parts that had been buried under tired routines.<br>The parts I didn’t have time to feel when I was always running.</p>

<p>This blog — these words — they’re not polished or perfect.<br>But they’re <em>mine</em>.<br>And they help me see where I’ve been,<br>and maybe where I’m going.</p>

<p>Today, I wrote about voting.<br>About the years I didn’t choose.<br>And the moment I finally decided to.</p>

<p>Nothing big has changed.<br>But something inside me feels… lighter.<br>Just one percent. Just one breath’s worth.</p>

<p>And maybe — if someone out there reads this and thinks,<br><em>“Maybe I’ll vote too…”</em></p>

<p>That would mean the world.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>✍️ Closing Words</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Generated-Image-June-22-2025-4_05PM.jpeg" alt="" class="wp-image-1816"/></figure>

<p>At first, I hesitated to write about politics.<br>Talking about elections can feel heavy — even risky.<br>Everyone has different views, and the topic often divides more than it connects.</p>

<p>But then I thought…<br><em>Is staying silent helping anyone?</em></p>

<p>For years, I avoided choosing.<br>Maybe that’s how we ended up with a future that feels harder to believe in.</p>

<p>Not because we didn’t care —<br>but because we were too tired, too afraid, or too stuck to act.</p>

<p>I used to think voting didn’t matter.<br>That nothing would change.</p>

<p>But now, I think differently.</p>

<p>My kids are grown.<br>I have more time to breathe.<br>And I’ve started thinking about what kind of world we’re handing over to them.</p>

<p>That’s why I decided to write this.<br>Not because I have the answers,<br>but because I finally have the words —<br>and I wanted to use them.</p>

<p>Writing with Chappy helped me hear my own voice again.<br>And choosing to share that voice, even quietly,<br>feels like a small but honest step toward change.</p>

<p>Voting may not fix everything.<br>But choosing something — anything — for yourself<br>might be where it all begins.</p>

<p>Thank you for reading.<br>Let’s keep choosing, in our own quiet ways.</p>
