<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Talking with the Dentist Before Extraction</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Google_AI_Studio_2025-09-19T08_11_26.079Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2293"/></figure>

<p>On the day of the extraction, I asked my dentist:</p>

<p>“I’m thinking of getting an implant for my first molar, but which tooth should I extract first?”</p>

<p>In my head, the plan was simple:<br><strong>“Remove the first molar → One implant solves the problem.”</strong></p>

<p>But the dentist’s answer was unexpected:</p>

<p>“Since periodontal disease has spread to your second molar as well, if you want an implant, you’ll need to remove two teeth. But first, it’s better to take out your wisdom tooth.”</p>

<p>…Wait, <strong>two teeth!?</strong></p>

<p>I had only prepared myself for one.<br>And to start with the wisdom tooth—the dreaded one that’s hard to pull out?</p>

<p>Inside my head, I was screaming:<br><strong>“No wayyy (tears)!”</strong></p>

<p>Honestly, I wasn’t just afraid of the pain.<br><strong>I was terrified of the cost.</strong><strong><br></strong> My wallet was crying louder than my heart.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Chicken Old Man on the Chopping Board</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Google_AI_Studio_2025-09-19T08_17_10.575Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2294"/></figure>

<p>Wisdom teeth = legendary pain.<br>I felt like a helpless fish on the chopping board.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>My heart was pounding.<br></li>

<li>My hands were sweaty.<br></li>

<li>My lips were trembling, even though I kept silent.<br></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Tears almost slipped out… I was a total chicken old man.</strong></p>

<p>To be honest, what scared me the most wasn’t the pain itself.<br>It was the <strong>sound of the instruments</strong> and the fear of <strong>“when will I hear that cracking sound?”</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Truth About Anesthesia and Extraction</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Google_AI_Studio_2025-09-19T08_19_24.915Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2295"/></figure>

<p>Then the treatment began.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>The anesthesia needle? <strong>Almost painless.</strong><strong><br></strong></li>

<li>Just a slight spreading sensation, nothing serious.<br></li>
</ul>

<p>And the extraction itself…</p>

<p>Yes, I heard a <strong>“crack”</strong> sound once and my heart jumped.<br>But that was only the sound of the tooth being shaved and loosened—<strong>no pain at all.</strong></p>

<p>👉 The dentist carefully shaved part of my sideways wisdom tooth and removed it in just <strong>5 minutes.</strong></p>

<p>“Huh? That’s it? No pain at all.”</p>

<p>It ended so quickly that I felt silly for worrying so much.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>No stitches<br></li>

<li>No major swelling<br></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Relief washed over me.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>My Options Going Forward</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Google_AI_Studio_2025-09-19T08_22_50.600Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2296"/></figure>

<p>After the extraction, I considered four possible paths:</p>

<ol class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Do Nothing (Leave As Is)</strong><strong><br></strong><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Pros: No cost<br></li>

<li>Cons: Must live with pain<br></li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Two Dental Implants</strong><strong><br></strong><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Pros: Most natural chewing ability<br></li>

<li>Cons: Expensive, long-term process (especially with periodontal treatment)<br></li>

<li>Cost: <strong>$2,000–$4,000 per tooth (≈ 300,000–600,000 yen)</strong><strong><br></strong></li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Insurance-Covered Partial Denture</strong><strong><br></strong><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Pros: Affordable<br></li>

<li>Cons: Metal clasps put stress on neighboring teeth<br></li>
</ul>
</li>

<li><strong>Non-Clasp Denture (Out-of-Pocket)</strong><strong><br></strong><ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Pros: Natural look / Lighter / Comfortable / No stress on weak teeth<br></li>

<li>Cons: <strong>About $700+ (≈ 100,000 yen)</strong> / May need replacement every few years<br></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Why I Lean Toward Non-Clasp Dentures</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Google_AI_Studio_2025-09-19T08_25_04.603Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2297"/></figure>

<p>The biggest reason I’m considering a <strong>non-clasp denture</strong> is simple:</p>

<p>👉 <strong>It doesn’t put extra stress on my already weak neighboring tooth.</strong></p>

<p>Traditional dentures use metal clasps, which can damage teeth over time.<br>With my neighbor tooth already loose from periodontal disease, I didn’t want to risk further harm.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>How Non-Clasp Dentures Work (and Are They Reliable?)</strong></h3>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Traditional partial dentures:</strong> Metal clasps hook onto healthy teeth<br></li>

<li><strong>Non-clasp dentures:</strong> Made of flexible resin that fits snugly against gums and surrounding teeth<br></li>
</ul>

<p>👉 Think of it as a <strong>blend between a soft, clear mouthguard and a denture.</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Nearly invisible when worn<br></li>

<li>Made from durable, medical-grade flexible resin<br></li>

<li>Comfortable to wear and remove<br></li>

<li>Fine for everyday eating, but not ideal for very hard foods<br></li>

<li>May need replacement every few years<br></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>In short:</strong><strong><br></strong> If you value <em>natural look &amp; comfort</em> → Non-clasp dentures are for you.<br>If you value <em>long-term chewing power</em> → Implants are the better choice.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Financial Worries and the Medical Expense Deduction</strong></h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Google_AI_Studio_2025-09-19T08_28_09.671Z.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-2298"/></figure>

<p>The elephant in the room: <strong>money.</strong><strong><br></strong> Implants and dentures aren’t cheap.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Dental implants in Japan typically cost <strong>$2,000–$4,000 per tooth (300,000–600,000 yen)</strong><strong><br></strong></li>

<li>Non-clasp dentures usually start from <strong>$700 (100,000 yen)</strong><strong><br></strong></li>
</ul>

<p>But here’s the good news:<br><strong>Both implants and non-clasp dentures can qualify for Japan’s medical expense deduction.</strong></p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>What Is the Medical Expense Deduction?</strong></h3>

<p>If your annual medical expenses exceed:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>$700 (100,000 yen)</strong>, or<br></li>

<li><strong>5% of your annual income (if under $14,000 / 2 million yen)</strong><strong><br></strong></li>
</ul>

<p>…you can claim part of it back through your tax return.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Eligible Treatments</strong></h3>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Implants (tests, surgery, materials)<br></li>

<li>Out-of-pocket dentures (non-clasp, metal plate, etc.)<br></li>

<li>Prosthetics for functional recovery<br></li>
</ul>

<p>👉 Cosmetic purposes (like whitening) are <em>not</em> covered.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Tips for Filing</strong></h3>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Keep all receipts and contracts<br></li>

<li>Transportation costs to the clinic may be deductible<br></li>

<li>Ask your dentist for a certificate if possible<br></li>
</ul>

<p>Knowing this system makes the financial burden <strong>much lighter.</strong></p>
