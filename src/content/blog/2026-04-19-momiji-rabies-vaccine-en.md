<h2 id="first-the-basics-rabies-vaccination-is-r" class="wp-block-heading">First, the Basics: Rabies Vaccination Is Required by Law in Japan</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/upload_3280-2.jpg" alt="Momiji in the parking lot, blissfully unaware of what awaits." class="wp-image-3993"/></figure>

<p>Rabies might feel like a distant concern if you live in Japan — but globally, it kills around 59,000 people a year, with 95% of cases in Asia and Africa.</p>
<p>Once symptoms appear, <strong>the fatality rate is nearly 100%</strong>. No effective treatment exists after onset.</p>
<p>Japan has had zero domestic rabies cases since 1957, thanks to the Rabies Prevention Act (enacted 1950), which mandates vaccination, registration, and ID tag requirements for all dog owners.</p>
<p>Three legal obligations every dog owner in Japan must follow:</p>
<ol class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>Dog registration</strong> (once in a lifetime)</li><li><strong>Annual rabies vaccination</strong></li><li><strong>Wear the registration tag and vaccination tag on the collar at all times</strong></li></ol>
<p>Failure to comply can result in a fine of up to ¥200,000 (approx. $1,333).</p>
<p>Vaccinations are available at municipal mass vaccination events (April–June) or at your local vet year-round. Typical cost: ¥2,500–3,100 (approx. $16.67–$20.67) for the shot, plus a ¥550 (approx. $3.67) tag issuance fee.</p>
<p>The tag color changes every year — 2026's color is red. Some municipalities now issue cute bone-shaped or paw-print-shaped tags.</p>

<h2 id="full-panic-mode" class="wp-block-heading">Full Panic Mode</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/upload_3286-1.jpg" alt="Momiji desperately trying to escape — she knew." class="wp-image-3998"/></figure>

<p>She went wild.</p>
<p>"She knew, didn't she..." I thought, struggling to hold her steady. We turned her around, the vet gave her the shot from behind.</p>
<p>Done. In seconds. She gave me this look like "something just happened," but it was already over.</p>
<p>The shot itself takes seconds — but the waiting is so much longer. Maybe that tense anticipation is the hardest part for dogs.</p>
<h2 id="what-to-watch-for-after-the-shot" class="wp-block-heading">What to Watch for After the Shot</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/upload_3283-1.jpg" alt="The vaccination event line — many owners gathered together." class="wp-image-3995"/></figure>

<p>When we got home, I kept a close eye on her.</p>
<p>The first 24 hours after vaccination are the key monitoring window.</p>
<p><strong>Anaphylaxis (severe allergic reaction) can occur within 30 minutes</strong> of the shot — so it's best to wait near the clinic for a while before heading home.</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list"><li><strong>No strenuous exercise on the day of the shot</strong> (no dog parks or long walks)</li><li><strong>No bathing for 3 days</strong> (to protect the injection site)</li><li><strong>If she seems low-energy, just wait 1–2 days</strong></li></ul>
<p>If you notice facial swelling, difficulty breathing, or fainting — get to a vet immediately.</p>
