<p>Hi, I'm Hiroshi.</p>

<p>For years I had a habit of talking myself out of things. "I could go, but… maybe not." The fear of disappointment felt safer than risking it.</p>

<p>I'd been doing exactly that with Hananomiya Park at Lake Yamanaka for over a decade.</p>

<p>What finally changed? I arrived at the hot spring one hour before it opened. Entirely my own fault. And that accidental hour of free time led me somewhere I should have gone years ago.</p>

<p>This is that story — plus everything you need to know before you visit.</p>

<p><strong>"Maybe I'll skip it" is almost always the wrong call.</strong></p>

<p>The waterfall sounded exactly like a real waterfall should. The triple waterwheel was exactly as impressive as its name implies. The lava cave underground was significantly scarier than it looks on video. And the ¥600 (approx. $4) admission was absolutely worth it.</p>

<h2 id="road">The luxury of thinking about nothing on the Doshu Road</h2>

<p>It was a weekday morning. I had nowhere in particular to be.</p>

<p>After a bowl of ramen, I got on my bike and headed for the Doshu Road — one of Japan's most scenic mountain routes in Kanagawa and Yamanashi prefectures.</p>

<p>The Doshu Road doesn't need a destination. Cold mountain air, one curve after another, the light filtering through the trees. Twist the throttle and your head empties out.</p>

<p>In my twenties, riding was about getting somewhere. In my fifties, <strong>the ride itself is the point.</strong> No hurry. No reason to rush. That's the quiet luxury of a weekday on a motorcycle.</p>

<p>Before I knew it, I was near Lake Yamanaka.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 id="onsen">Arriving at the hot spring one hour early</h2>

<p>"I'm already here — might as well soak in the hot spring before heading home."</p>

<p>I rode to Beniuji no Yu, the hot spring near Lake Yamanaka.</p>

<p>I arrived. Turned off the engine. Read the sign.</p>

<p><strong>"Open from 11:00 on weekdays."</strong></p>

<p>I checked my phone. <strong>10:00.</strong></p>

<p>……</p>

<p>Nobody's fault but mine. A five-second Google Maps check would have told me this. I just didn't check.</p>

<p>I sat on my bike for a minute, doing nothing. "What now," I asked myself. Nobody in the parking lot answered.</p>

<p>I started the engine and rode around. That's when I spotted a sign: <strong>"Yamanakako Hananomiya Park."</strong></p>

<p>Six attractions for ¥600 (approx. $4). Worth it. I'll explain with my body later.</p>

<h2 id="nyuen">The day I finally entered the park I'd skipped for ten years</h2>

<p>I'd known this park existed for years. Every time I rode through the area, I saw the signs. Every time I thought "maybe next time" and kept going.</p>

<p>For over a decade.</p>

<p><strong>The reason my decade-long streak of skipping it finally ended was that I arrived at a hot spring one hour too early.</strong> Not exactly a heroic motivation — but that's the truth.</p>

<p>I paid ¥300 (approx. $2) for parking and walked toward the entrance.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 id="free">Why I stopped walking in the free zone</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-entrance.jpg" alt="The wooden bridge at the entrance of Hananomiya Park with a sign reading 'Welcome to Hananomiya Park'" class="wp-image-6302"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The entrance bridge. Crossing it shifts something — you're in park mode now</figcaption></figure>

<p>There's a small wooden bridge at the entrance, with a blue sign: "Welcome to Hananomiya Park."</p>

<p>The moment I crossed it, the road noise disappeared. In its place: a faint sound of running water somewhere in the distance.</p>

<p>"Okay. This is a real place."</p>

<p>That was my honest first impression.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-fuji-field.jpg" alt="Mount Fuji visible beyond farmland with clouds partially covering the peak" class="wp-image-6306"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Fuji through the clouds, beyond the farmland. Just that silhouette was enough.</figcaption></figure>

<p>Past the entrance bridge, the farmland opened up. Mount Fuji was visible through a gap in the clouds — just the outline, the silhouette.</p>

<p>That was enough.</p>

<p>📷 <strong>Photo tip</strong>: Mount Fuji is visible from the farmland area near the entrance. Fuji tends to cloud over as the morning goes on — check early.</p>

<h2 id="ticket">What I thought while pulling out my wallet — and what I thought after</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-ticket.jpg" alt="Sign at the paid zone entrance showing admission prices: Adults 600 yen, dogs free" class="wp-image-6307"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">I paused here for a moment. Then I paid. Right call.</figcaption></figure>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-seiryu-gate.jpg" alt="The entrance building to Seiryu no Sato, with a large sign showing the name of the paid zone" class="wp-image-6308"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Seiryu no Sato entrance. This is where the paid zone begins</figcaption></figure>

<p>I paused at the entrance sign. "Seiryu no Sato &amp; Floral Dome Furara — Adults ¥600 (approx. $4)."</p>

<p>Reaching for my wallet, I thought: <em>"¥600 (approx. $4) for what was supposed to be killing time…"</em></p>

<p>I paid. <strong>"That was the right call."</strong> That's what I thought after.</p>

<p>Skipping the paid zone means skipping the Triple Waterwheel, Myojin Falls, the Floral Dome, and the lava cave. Everything that comes next in this article. <strong>That's too much to leave on the table.</strong></p>

<p>The first thing I noticed entering the paid zone was the flower fields.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-nemophila.jpg" alt="Close-up of nemophila flowers in pale blue-purple. In a field, they look like they're made of sky.</figcaption></figure>

<p><strong>Nemophila</strong> — masses of small blue-purple flowers across the ground. One flower is easy to overlook. Together, they produce a color that blurs into the sky.</p>

<p>I crouched down to look closely. Five petals, detailed markings at the center.</p>

<p>Twenty-year-old me never would have crouched to look at a flower. <strong>Somewhere in my fifties, I started being able to actually see small things.</strong></p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-white-flower.jpg" alt="A field of small white flowers stretching to the horizon" class="wp-image-6304"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">White flowers filling the field. From a distance, the ground looks like it's floating.</figcaption></figure>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-white-flower2.jpg" alt="Wide-angle view of the white flower field with green trees in the background" class="wp-image-6305"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">All of this is inside the paid zone — and you see it right after entering.</figcaption></figure>

<p>📷 <strong>Photo tip</strong>: Right after entering the paid zone, you can shoot nemophila with Fuji in the background. Crouch down, put nemophila in the foreground. Fuji tends to cloud over as the morning goes on — shoot this early.</p>

<h2 id="suisha">The triple waterwheel made me say "oh" out loud, alone</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-suisha-sign.jpg" alt="Information board for the Triple Waterwheel: diameter approximately 6m per wheel, flow rate approximately 250L/minute" class="wp-image-6309"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Diameter 6m, flow rate 250L/min. The numbers are impressive. The real thing is more so.</figcaption></figure>

<p>The first thing I saw entering the paid zone was the <strong>Triple Waterwheel</strong>. I walked toward it thinking "waterwheel, sure." Low expectations.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-suisha-all.jpg" alt="The triple waterwheel in full view — three large wooden wheels turning side by side" class="wp-image-6310"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Three wheels. All turning. The moment I saw it, "oh" came out of my mouth</figcaption></figure>

<p>I saw it. <strong>They were big.</strong></p>

<p>Three large wooden wheels, side by side. All of them turning. Scooping water, letting it fall, going around again. The sound of the water hitting the surface below.</p>

<p>"Oh," I said out loud. Nobody around to hear it. Weekday morning.</p>

<p>Nobody there — and I still made a sound involuntarily. That's how much it exceeded my expectations. The name "Triple Waterwheel" is exactly what it is. Three wheels. Enormous. Turning. No exaggeration in the name at all.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-suisha-close.jpg" alt="Close-up of one of the waterwheel paddles scooping water" class="wp-image-6311"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The moment it scoops, spills, and turns again. <h2 id="fulala">Floral Dome Furara: a different climate inside a mountain</h2>

<p>I entered the all-weather greenhouse, Floral Dome Furara.</p>

<p>The moment I stepped inside, <strong>the air changed.</strong> It was warm. Outside was 1,000m elevation in May — cool enough to want a jacket. Inside was humid, warm, and sweet-smelling. <strong>The dense sweetness of tropical flowers.</strong></p>

<p>Rain or clouds outside: irrelevant. In here, you're somewhere else entirely. <strong>This is one of the most reliable "weather-proof" spots I know.</strong></p>

<h2 id="taki">Standing in front of Myojin Falls, thinking nothing at all</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-myojin-sign.jpg" alt="Information board for Myojin Falls: height approximately 10m, width approximately 80m" class="wp-image-6316"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">"Height approx. 10m, width approx. 80m." I read this and braced myself a little.</figcaption></figure>

<p>Of everything in the paid zone, <strong>Myojin Falls was the highlight.</strong></p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-myojin-taki-v3.jpg?v=3" alt="Myojin Falls — wide rock face with multiple streams of white water falling into a pale green pool below" class="wp-image-6317"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Myojin Falls. 80 meters wide. <p>It was wider than I'd imagined.</p>

<p>Multiple streams of white water fell down a broad rock face. The pool at the bottom was a pale green, the rock pattern visible through the water. Only the sound of water.</p>

<p>Not a rush or a roar — surprisingly quiet for something that wide. Clear and steady.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-taki-suisha.jpg" alt="A view showing both the waterfall and the waterwheel through green trees" class="wp-image-6318"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">From this spot you can see both the falls and the waterwheel. ¥600 (approx. $4) well spent.</figcaption></figure>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-iwa.jpg" alt="The Iwashimizu Falls area — mossy rocks and trees" class="wp-image-6319"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Iwashimizu Falls area. Quiet, damp, and completely still.</figcaption></figure>

<p>I stood there for a while. Thinking nothing at all.</p>

<p>The only two times today I stopped thinking were: while riding the Doshu Road, and while standing in front of this waterfall. <strong>That was the real luxury of the day.</strong></p>

<p>Twenty-year-old me would have been grabbing more photos. Thirty-year-old me would have been planning the next stop. Fifty-year-old me just stood there, listening to the water. That was enough.</p>

<h2 id="yogan">In the lava cave, I quietly asked myself if I was okay</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-yogan-sign.jpg" alt="Sign for the Lava Tree Mold Underground Observation Zone: 'Lava Tree Mold'" class="wp-image-6320"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">"Lava Tree Mold." I wasn't scared yet at this point.</figcaption></figure>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/yamanakako-hananomiya-park-yogan-desc.jpg" alt="Detailed information board explaining the formation of lava tree molds from the eruptions of Mount Fuji" class="wp-image-6321"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">I read this, thought "fascinating," and walked in. The regret came shortly after.</figcaption></figure>

<p>The Lava Tree Mold Underground Observation Zone. When Mount Fuji erupted, lava encased the trees. The trees burned away, leaving cylindrical tunnels underground. Those are lava tree molds.</p>

<p>"That sounds interesting." I walked in alone. <strong>This was my mistake.</strong></p>

<p>Three steps inside the entrance. <strong>Dark.</strong> Five steps. <strong>Darker.</strong> A chill rising from the floor. The sound — completely different from outside. Completely still. Cold enough that my breath almost showed.</p>

<p>I took out my phone and started filming. On screen, everything looked <strong>surprisingly bright.</strong> But with my own eyes: dim. "Why does it look so much lighter on video?"</p>

<p>I kept going. Kept getting a little more unsettled.</p>

<p>Nobody here. No sound. Cold. Dark. <strong>A man in his fifties, alone in an underground lava cave.</strong></p>

<p>Then, out of nowhere, a thought: "Wait, is it okay to film in here?" Probably fine. But "probably" wasn't good enough anymore. I wanted to check with someone. Nobody was there.</p>

<p>I caught myself whispering: "Am I okay?"</p>

<p>To myself. Nobody heard. The rock walls reflected it slightly. That made it worse.</p>

<p>I turned around. Fast-walked to the exit. Stepped out into the daylight and <strong>felt genuinely relieved.</strong> By myself. Relieved, by myself.</p>

<p>Go in knowing that, and it becomes an interesting experience. Go in blind, alone, and you might end up whispering to yourself in the dark. <strong>If possible, don't go in alone.</strong> Two people: funny story. One person: possible horror experience.</p>

<h2 id="matome">Final thoughts: The days that don't go to plan</h2>

<p>Riding home on the Doshu Road, I kept thinking: "That was genuinely good."</p>

<p>I could still hear the thin, clear sound of Myojin Falls somewhere in my memory.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p>An accidental hour — arriving at the hot spring too early — sent me into a park I'd skipped for a decade.</p>

<p>I said "oh" out loud in front of a waterwheel with nobody around. I stood at a waterfall and stopped thinking. I scared myself in an underground cave and whispered my own name.</p>

<p><strong>All of it started with something going wrong.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p>I've noticed something in my fifties.</p>

<p><strong>The days that don't go to plan are the ones I remember most.</strong></p>

<p>Days where everything worked perfectly, where the plan held — I forget those. Days where something broke, but it still worked out — those stay with me.</p>

<p>Arriving too early at a hot spring led me to a park I should have visited years ago. <strong>"Maybe I'll skip it" is almost always the wrong call.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p>Riding home, I wanted to tell someone about the day.</p>

<p>I thought about who I'd tell. And then felt a little lonely for a moment.</p>

<p>So I wrote it here instead.</p>

<p>If this got you thinking "maybe I'll check it out" — that's all I needed.</p>
