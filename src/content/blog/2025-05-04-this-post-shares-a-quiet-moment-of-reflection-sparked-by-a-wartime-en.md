<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>A smiling face in a wartime photo—<br>It wasn’t just a story from the past.<br>It felt like a quiet question for those of us living today.</p></blockquote>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>May 3rd, 2025 – Constitution Memorial Day. A headline on Yahoo! News caught my eye.</strong></p>

<p><strong>"The Untold Smile, the Remembered Stern Look – Battleship Yamato and the Memories of War."</strong></p>

<p><strong>In 1945, during a suicide mission, the Battleship Yamato was sunk. Of its 3,332 crew members, 3,056 perished. In the photo were young men, smiling freely.</strong></p>

<p><strong>These young men were far younger than I am now in my 50s. Why did such promising lives have to be sacrificed?</strong></p>

<p><strong>That thought fills me with frustration and sorrow.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>"The Japanese Constitution was made in a week." That might sound surprising.</strong></p>

<p><strong>In fact, GHQ's Civil Affairs Bureau drafted the proposal in just nine days in February 1946. Japan’s government revised and approved it, and the Constitution was enacted on May 3, 1947.</strong></p>

<p><strong>Our peace, freedom, and human rights were rooted in those early, rushed days.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>Honestly, I’m not well-versed in politics or constitutional law. But whenever I hear the voices of those who died in war—or those who lived long after—I realize how precious my peaceful days with ramen and walks with Momiji are.</strong></p>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full is-resized"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/570386b54b3cb2771f189ff9bf9b75d6.png" alt="" class="wp-image-347" style="width:441px;height:auto"/></figure>

<p><strong>Today’s world is full of uncertainties—climate change, economic struggles, division.</strong></p>

<p><strong>But people in wartime must have lived in fear too, doing their best with what they had.</strong></p>

<p><strong>So maybe we too can live each day earnestly, doing what we can in our own way.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p><strong>If someone asks me, "What can I do?" this is what I’d say:</strong></p>

<p><strong>Learn history. Value peace. Pass that spirit on to the next generation.</strong></p>

<p><strong>That might be the form of quiet compassion we can offer.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-full is-resized"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/a131dade5bd1b2ff23467955af15756d.png" alt="" class="wp-image-348" style="width:496px;height:auto"/></figure>

<p><strong>Today, the sky was clear. In the spring sunlight, Momiji napped peacefully.</strong></p>

<p><strong>What we must protect, I believe, is this very everyday life.</strong></p>

<p><strong>With that feeling, I wrote this post today.</strong></p>
