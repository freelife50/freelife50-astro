<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The Zoo Was Rejected. Hakone Museum of Art It Was.</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":4045,"sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/02_entrance_sign.jpg" alt="Hakone Museum of Art entrance sign" class="wp-image-4045"/></figure>

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I've been to Hakone a few times, but I'd always skipped the museum. &#8220;Too quiet, I'll fall asleep&#8221; — that was my honest assumption.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Today, though, my wife had the final say.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Before I get into the experience, here&#8217;s the practical info:</p>

<!-- wp:heading -->
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">A Place That Feels Like Old Japan</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":4057,"sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/03_building_teahouse.jpg" alt="Hakone Museum of Art retro Japanese building" class="wp-image-4057"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Stepping inside, the first thing that surprised me was the atmosphere.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Stone paths. Bamboo fences. Wooden buildings worn smooth with age.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>It felt like stepping into the private villa of some old-money family from the Meiji or Taisho era. I half-expected a servant to appear and ask if I&#8217;d like tea.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The museum opened in 1952 — the oldest private museum in Hakone. The founder, Mokichi Okada (1882–1955), designed the main building himself. The distinctive Chinese-style blue-tiled roof stands out beautifully against all the green.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":4059,"sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/09_garden_rocks.jpg" alt="Shinsenkyo garden rocks and landscape" class="wp-image-4059"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The garden, called <em>Shinsenkyo</em>, covers about 33,000 square meters (roughly 8 acres). In 2021, it was designated a National Place of Scenic Beauty.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Rocks, buildings, red azaleas — layered together in a way that felt almost painted.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:separator -->
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<!-- /wp:separator -->

<!-- wp:heading -->
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Honestly, the Pottery Didn&#8217;t Grab Me</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":4046,"sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/04_exhibit_pots.jpg" alt="Hakone Museum of Art ceramic exhibit" class="wp-image-4046"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The exhibition halls were filled with ancient ceramics — from Jomon-era pottery to Edo-period porcelain.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Four huge old jars lined the window wall. The mountains framed perfectly behind them. That view was genuinely beautiful.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>But honestly? The pottery itself didn&#8217;t do much for me.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I walked through thinking, &#8220;Huh. Old pots.&#8221; My wife, on the other hand, studied every piece carefully. If ceramics are your thing, this place is probably a paradise.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:separator -->
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<!-- /wp:separator -->

<!-- wp:heading -->
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Wait — Hakone Has a <em>Daimonji</em> Fire Festival?</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":4047,"sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/05_daimonji_mountain.jpg" alt="Myojogatake mountain Daimonji Hakone" class="wp-image-4047"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Up on the second floor, my wife pointed out the window: &#8220;That mountain — apparently they do a <em>Daimonji</em> fire ceremony there.&#8221;</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I looked closer. There it was — the shape of the kanji 大 (<em>dai</em>, meaning &#8220;large&#8221;) carved into the mountain&#8217;s tree line.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I always thought <em>Daimonji</em> was a Kyoto thing. Turns out Hakone has had its own version since 1921.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The mountain is called <em>Myojogatake</em> (924m). Every year on August 16th, a massive bonfire is lit in the shape of 大 — with the horizontal stroke alone measuring 108 meters.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The building was designed so that the mountain sits perfectly in view from the second-floor windows. It&#8217;s a traditional Japanese garden technique called <em>shakkei</em> — &#8220;borrowed scenery.&#8221;</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:separator -->
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<!-- /wp:separator -->

<!-- wp:heading -->
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The People Crouching in the Moss Garden Stayed with Me</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":4048,"sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/06_gardener_work.jpg" alt="Gardeners working in Hakone moss garden" class="wp-image-4048"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Walking through the garden, I came across a wide carpet of moss.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>A few staff members were crouching low, working quietly in the middle of it.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>&#8220;What are you doing?&#8221; I asked.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>&#8220;Pulling weeds,&#8221; one of them told me, with a small smile.<br>&#8220;The garden&#8217;s so large — it&#8217;s a lot of work.&#8221;</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":4058,"sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/08_moss_closeup.jpg" alt="Close-up of moss in Hakone garden" class="wp-image-4058"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The moss garden contains around 130 species of moss.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Because different species require different levels of moisture and light, neither machines nor chemicals can be used. Every weed has to be identified by hand and removed without disturbing the moss around it.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>This isn&#8217;t just a practical decision — it&#8217;s a philosophy. The museum&#8217;s founder advocated for natural farming methods, and that principle still guides how the garden is maintained today.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Leave it alone for a few weeks, and the whole thing reverts to wild hillside. So every day, someone crouches down, removes each unwanted plant by hand, and checks how the moss is doing.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I watched them for a while. I didn&#8217;t say much. But I was genuinely moved.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:separator -->

<!-- wp:heading -->
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">The Beauty That Human Hands Pass Down</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":4080,"sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/10_garden_old_tree_FIXED.jpg" alt="An ancient gnarled tree rooted in the moss garden at Hakone Museum of Art, tended for over 70 years" class="wp-image-4080"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The walk through the garden left me thinking about one thing.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Every beautiful place has someone behind it, doing quiet, unglamorous work.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I wasn&#8217;t interested in the pottery. But I was deeply moved by the people maintaining this garden, day after day, for decades.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Buildings, gardens — without care, they deteriorate fast. The same is true of houses. A home nobody tends to becomes a ruin almost overnight.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The fact that this museum has stood here for over 70 years is because people kept showing up and doing the work.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I thought: long after I&#8217;m gone, this building — worn wood and all — will probably still be here, passed down by hands I&#8217;ll never know.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The breeze was cool and clear.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
