<p>Hi, I'm Hiroshi.</p>

<p>It's overcast. Foggy, actually. But my wife is in full gear anyway.</p>

<p>Sun hat, face mask, sunglasses, long sleeves, gloves. She walked up to the Mt. Nokogiriyama ropeway station looking like she was about to summit Everest. Rain or shine, my wife's "Tutankhamun Mode" is non-negotiable. I've made my peace with it.</p>

<p>Our destination today: Nihon-ji Temple on Mt. Nokogiriyama, Chiba Prefecture.</p>

<p>A few weeks ago, I visited the Nagoyama Bussharitou in Himeji and felt like I'd stumbled into Gandhara. Now Chiba pulled the same trick on me. Japan, apparently, is full of Gandharas. (<a href="/nagoyama-bussharitou-himeji-en/">Read the Himeji Gandhara story here</a>)</p>

<p>We also brought our Shiba Inu, Momiji — who by the end of the day had nearly achieved enlightenment through sheer exhaustion.</p>

<p>Chiba's Gandhara is the real deal. I sprained my ankle. But I want to go back.</p>

<h2 id="fees-and-access-first" class="wp-block-heading">Fees and Access — Let's Sort That Out First</h2>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-entrance-sign.jpg" alt="Nihon-ji Temple entrance gate with signboard reading Japan's largest cliff-carved Buddha" class="wp-image-6000"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The entrance to Nihon-ji Temple — admission starts here</figcaption></figure>

<p>Before anything else, let's clear up the cost question. Mt. Nokogiriyama has three separate things going on: the mountain itself (free to hike), Nihon-ji Temple (admission fee), and the ropeway (separate fare). Understanding these separately saves confusion at the gate.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table><thead><tr><th>Item</th><th>Price</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Ropeway round trip (adult)</td><td>$8 USD</td></tr><tr><td>Ropeway (small dog, one way)</td><td>$2 USD</td></tr><tr><td>Nihon-ji Temple admission (adult)</td><td>$5 USD</td></tr><tr><td>Ropeway parking lot (weekends/holidays)</td><td>$4 USD</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<p>Pets: Dogs under 10kg can ride the ropeway for a one-way fee of about $2. Momiji (Shiba Inu) made the weight limit — just barely.</p>

<p><strong>Hours:</strong> Nihon-ji Temple 9:00 AM–4:00 PM (last entry 3:00 PM), Ropeway 9:00 AM–5:00 PM</p>

<p><strong>Access:</strong> From Hamakanaya Station (JR Uchibou Line), it's about an 8–9 minute walk to the ropeway base station. From Tokyo Station, allow roughly 2 hours and about $13 USD in train fare.</p>

<p>Pets are welcome inside the temple grounds on a leash. That said, the terrain is steep and uneven — sturdy shoes are essential. (I'll explain why from personal experience shortly.)</p>

<p><strong>How long does it take?</strong> From the ropeway base station, the full circuit — Great Buddha, 1,500 Rakan corridor, Hell's Lookout, Hyakushaku Kannon, Laputa Wall, and back down to the base — takes roughly 2 to 2.5 hours. Build in extra time if your footwear is questionable. And a little extra if you sprain your ankle, which is, admittedly, a specific scenario.</p>

<p>"Well, this is atmospheric in its own way," I told myself, and moved on.</p>

<h2 id="welcome-to-chibas-gandhara" class="wp-block-heading">Welcome to Chiba's Gandhara — The Great Buddha</h2>

<p>We walked from the summit station toward the temple. Admission to Nihon-ji costs about $5 per adult — this is where the main event begins.</p>

<p>A quick history note: Nihon-ji was founded in 725 AD by the monk Gyoki, and 2025 marks its 1,300th anniversary. The temple is now a Soto Zen institution. Minamoto no Yoritomo — the founder of Japan's first shogunate — once prayed here for victory in battle and later rebuilt the main hall in 1181.</p>

<p>At 10:56 AM we reached the approach to the Great Buddha. At 11:00 AM sharp, we arrived at Daibutsu Plaza.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-daibutsu-full.jpg" alt="Nihon-ji Great Buddha, a massive stone cliff-carved sitting Buddha surrounded by greenery" class="wp-image-6004"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">That's the word for it: massive</figcaption></figure>

<p>It's massive.</p>

<p>Standing 31.05 meters tall — more than twice the height of the famous Nara Great Buddha at 14.98 meters — this is a magaibutsu: a Buddha carved directly into the living rock. The original was completed in 1783; the current restoration dates to the Showa era.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-daibutsu-closeup.jpg" alt="Close-up of the Nihon-ji Great Buddha's face, peaceful expression" class="wp-image-6005"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A gentle face, even through the fog</figcaption></figure>

<p>The expression is serene. Even wrapped in fog, you can feel it.</p>

<p>And then: the shot of the day.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-momiji-daibutsu.jpg" alt="Shiba Inu Momiji sitting in front of the Great Buddha with tongue out, looking happy" class="wp-image-6006"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">1,300 years of history vs. Momiji (age 3). Tongue out, no contest</figcaption></figure>

<p>Thirteen centuries of Buddhist history behind her, and she's grinning like she just found a great stick. Honestly the best photo I've ever taken.</p>

<h2 id="the-sprained-ankle-story" class="wp-block-heading">How I Sprained My Ankle in the 1,500 Rakan Corridor</h2>

<p>From Daibutsu Plaza, we headed into the Higashi Sengo Rakan — the corridor of 1,553 stone figures.</p>

<p>The story behind them: in 1780, the head priest Koga Uden commissioned sculptor Ono Jingoro Hidenori and 27 apprentices to carve every single figure over 21 years. Each one has a different face. Some are laughing, some stern, some look half-asleep.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-momiji-steps.jpg" alt="Shiba Inu Momiji bounding up stone steps at Nihon-ji Temple" class="wp-image-6007"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Momiji takes the steps at full speed. Four legs: built for this</figcaption></figure>

<p>Momiji charged up the stone steps without hesitation. Dogs have an unfair structural advantage on terrain like this, and I say that with complete seriousness.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-cave-passage.jpg" alt="Shiba Inu Momiji walking through a narrow cave-like passage flanked by rock walls at Nihon-ji" class="wp-image-6008"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A narrow passage through the rock. Dim, stone-carved faces everywhere — genuinely otherworldly</figcaption></figure>

<p>The corridor closes in on both sides, with stone figures tucked into every crevice and overhang. It felt like walking through a different world entirely.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-stone-buddha.jpg" alt="Stone Buddha figure nestled carefully in a rock crevice at Nihon-ji Temple" class="wp-image-6009"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Carved in the 1800s, still here. That's the definition of enduring</figcaption></figure>

<p>Then, at around 11:30 AM, on a completely ordinary-looking slope, I slipped.</p>

<p>Sprained my ankle.</p>

<p>Not on the stairs. Not on the roped section. Just on a normal inclined path. My wife, sealed in full Tutankhamun Mode, was fine. I twisted my ankle on a moderate slope in regular sneakers.</p>

<p>I could still walk, so I kept going — but every step for the rest of the day came with a dull throb in my ankle.</p>

<p><strong>Lesson: proper footwear is non-negotiable here.</strong> You don't need hiking boots, but thin-soled sneakers are genuinely risky. Trust the person who found out the hard way. Check out more of <a href="/this-post-shares-a-peaceful-spring-outing-to-kannonzaki-with-my-wife-en/">our dog-friendly walks around the Kanto region</a> for comparison.</p>

<h2 id="hells-lookout-in-the-fog" class="wp-block-heading">Hell's Lookout — The Fog Made It Scarier</h2>

<p>Via Fudo Waterfall, we reached the summit observation area — Jigoku Nozoki, or "Hell's Lookout" — at 11:47 AM.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-jigoku-overview.jpg" alt="Hell's Lookout at Mt. Nokogiriyama — a dramatic rock ledge jutting out over the valley, viewed from a distance" class="wp-image-6010"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">That's the ledge. Even from here, it looks serious</figcaption></figure>

<p>This is the famous one: a rock ledge that juts out dramatically over the valley below. On a clear day, you can see Tokyo Bay spread out beneath you.</p>

<p>Today: pure white fog below. You couldn't see the bottom.</p>

<p>Counterintuitively, that made it scarier. Standing on the edge and not being able to see how far down it goes produces a specific kind of dread that's actually worse than seeing the drop clearly. I was genuinely more nervous than I expected.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-jigoku-momiji.jpg" alt="Tutankhamun-Mode wife and Shiba Inu Momiji standing on Hell's Lookout ledge in the fog" class="wp-image-6011"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Tutankhamun and Shiba Inu, standing in the fog. Actually kind of a great photo</figcaption></figure>

<p>My wife and Momiji stood at the edge. The full Tutankhamun gear against a wall of white fog was, I have to admit, a striking image. Other visitors were probably wondering who she was.</p>

<h2 id="hyakushaku-kannon-and-laputa-wall" class="wp-block-heading">The Hyakushaku Kannon and the Laputa Wall</h2>

<p>At 12:09 PM: the Hyakushaku Kannon.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-hyakushaku-full.jpg" alt="The Hyakushaku Kannon at Nihon-ji, a 30-meter standing Buddha figure carved into the old quarry cliff face" class="wp-image-6012"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">About 30 meters tall. You'll crane your neck</figcaption></figure>

<p>Carved into the face of the old quarry cliff, this standing figure is approximately 30 meters tall. It was carved between 1960 and 1966 — a modern work, but no less impressive for it. Six years of hand-carving into living rock.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-hyakushaku-face.jpg" alt="Close-up of the Hyakushaku Kannon's face, a calm and warm expression" class="wp-image-6013"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">A different expression from the Great Buddha — warmer, somehow</figcaption></figure>

<p>You have to lean all the way back to see the full figure. The scale drives home just how much work went into it. Standing right next to it, you get a very clear sense of how small you are — not just physically, but in some harder-to-define way. Maybe that's what reverence feels like. Or maybe I just needed lunch.</p>

<p>Then, at around 12:05 PM, we passed through the old quarry zone — what locals and visitors have come to call the "Laputa Wall."</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-laputa-wall.jpg" alt="The dramatic vertical quarry cliff face at Mt. Nokogiriyama, known as the Laputa Wall, with clean cut stone surfaces" class="wp-image-6014"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">The Laputa Wall. Human hands made this — and now it's a landscape</figcaption></figure>

<figure class="wp-block-embed is-type-video" style="text-align:center;">
<div style="width:315px;height:560px;margin:0 auto;overflow:hidden;">
<iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/DMZXRMcWleA" style="width:100%;height:100%;border:0;display:block;" allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture; web-share" allowfullscreen loading="lazy"></iframe>

<p>The geological backstory: the summit area of Mt. Nokogiriyama contains Boshu-ishi, a type of tuffaceous sandstone that is easy to cut but fire-resistant. Quarrying began in the Edo period and only ended in 1985. The stone was used in the construction of Yokohama Port, island fortifications, and much more.</p>

<p>The "sawtooth" silhouette that gives the mountain its name — Nokogiri means "saw" — is literally the result of all that stone cutting. The boundaries between natural landscape and human intervention are completely blurred here, and that's what makes it unlike anywhere else in the Kanto region.</p>

<p>There's also an AR marker showing the quarry workers in action. Mine loaded. Genuinely interesting.</p>

<p>At 12:15 PM we headed to the Kanaya descent gate and started down the mountain.</p>

<h2 id="the-croquette-shop-where-yoshii-came" class="wp-block-heading">The Croquette Shop Where a Rock Star Apparently Showed Up</h2>

<p>We reached the bottom at 1:00 PM.</p>

<p>My ankle was aching. I was hungry. And then I spotted a sign: "Sassei Butcher Shop — Croquettes, $0.65 each."</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-croquette.jpg" alt="A tray of freshly made croquettes at Sassei Butcher Shop priced at 100 yen each" class="wp-image-6015"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">They were cold. I didn't care. They were excellent</figcaption></figure>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/nokogiriyama-croquette-shop.jpg" alt="Sassei Butcher Shop exterior with shop curtain and Momiji resting by the door" class="wp-image-6016"/><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Sassei Butcher Shop. Momiji claimed the sidewalk</figcaption></figure>

<p>Cold croquettes after a mountain hike hit differently. Genuinely good.</p>

<p>Then I noticed something on the shop's display wall: photos and a write-up indicating that Koji Kikkawa — one of Japan's most iconic rock musicians — had visited this exact shop.</p>

<p>Let me process that for a moment.</p>

<p>Koji Kikkawa. Japan's actual rock legend. I hiked through fog, saw Japan's largest stone Buddha, stood on Hell's Lookout, sprained my ankle on a completely normal slope, and the day ends with a Koji Kikkawa celebrity sighting at a croquette shop. This is a lot of information for one afternoon.</p>

<p>I told my wife. She was still in full Tutankhamun Mode. She said "Oh, really." And looked back at her phone. That was the entire reaction. A rock legend has graced this very establishment and that's what I got. I've made my peace with it.</p>

<p>Momiji, meanwhile, was collapsed on the sidewalk in front of the shop. By the last stretch of the hike she'd been moving at half speed, clearly running on fumes. At the Great Buddha she was bouncy and tongue-out happy. By the time we got to the croquette shop, she was doing her best impression of someone who has achieved complete detachment from the physical world. For more of our dog-friendly walking adventures, <a href="/a-wild-moment-in-okutama-my-dog-momiji-a-line-of-monkeys-and-a-en/">here's the Okutama story</a>.</p>

<p>Crowds peak around 11:00 AM on long weekends — parking lots can fill up. Arriving by 9:00 AM on weekends is the safe call. The ropeway suspends service in bad weather, so check current status before you go.</p>

<p>Footwear: running shoes minimum, light hiking shoes preferred. The stairs and rock paths get slippery, especially after rain. Water: bring your own — vending machines are limited to specific points on the route.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<p>Mt. Nokogiriyama's Nihon-ji Temple rewards you whether you do the whole circuit or just stop at Hell's Lookout. But doing it all — the Great Buddha, the Rakan corridor, the Lookout, the Hyakushaku Kannon, the Laputa Wall — gives you something few other half-day trips can match: the feeling that one small mountain contains way more than it has any right to.</p>

<p>We had zero visibility for views. I still came away satisfied. Living in the Kanto region and not having been here before felt like a genuine oversight. Next time, I'm coming on a clear day to actually see Tokyo Bay — and wearing proper hiking shoes.</p>

<p>On the drive home, Momiji was out cold in the back seat. My wife sat in the passenger seat, still in Tutankhamun Mode, scrolling her phone in silence. I drove with a mildly throbbing ankle and thought about croquettes.</p>

<p>The ankle healed by the next morning. The croquettes were genuinely excellent. We did not meet Koji Kikkawa. That was our day.</p>
