<p><strong>Read time:</strong> About 10 minutes</p>

<p>Feeling a little worn out lately?</p>
<p>Staring at your phone all day until your shoulders lock up, your head feels heavy, and your heart gets tired before your body does. I&#8217;ve been there too.</p>
<p>Today I want to share a small day trip I took to <strong>Ninomiya, Kanagawa</strong> — a coastal town about 80 minutes by train from Tokyo.</p>
<p>All I did was park at Ninomiya Fruit Park and walk down to Sodegaura Beach with my dog. Nothing fancy. But on the way home, my shoulders felt a little lighter. That&#8217;s the honest report.</p>
<p><strong>Who this post is for</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>People who want a small escape, but not a big trip</li>
<li>Dog owners looking for an easy half-day walk</li>
<li>Travelers who want a bit of sea <em>and</em> a bit of green in one visit</li>
<li>Anyone who hates paying for parking (me too)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>The quiet takeaway</strong></p>
<p><strong>A just-right hike and an easy seaside walk — on the same path.</strong><br />That&#8217;s what Ninomiya quietly offers.</p>
<p><strong>A &#8220;just right&#8221; distance can be quiet medicine for the mind.</strong><br />That&#8217;s how I felt. Not a rule. Just my impression.</p>
<hr>

<p>The park is planted with pears, grapes, and persimmons. Here&#8217;s something I didn&#8217;t know until I looked it up: this land used to be the <strong>Kanagawa Prefectural Horticulture Experiment Station</strong>. For decades, researchers here bred new varieties of fruit trees. What you&#8217;re walking through now is basically a living museum of Japanese modern agriculture.</p>
<p>I didn&#8217;t know any of that when I pulled into the lot. I just thought, &#8220;huh, a fruit park, sure.&#8221; Learning the history afterward made the morning feel a bit richer.</p>
<h2>My dog Momiji, cranked to eleven (again)</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3083.jpg" alt="Momiji walking the trail" class="wp-image-3083"/></figure>
<p>My dog, Momiji. The moment the car door opens, her tail turns into a metronome.</p>
<p>She always knows when the ocean is near. Nose, ears, sixth sense — I have no idea which. But she pulls forward, forward, forward.</p>
<p>&#8220;Hey, easy there. My knees are not as young as yours.&#8221;<br />She does not care about my knees.</p>

<h2>The walk from the park to the beach</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3087.jpg" alt="Scenery on the way to Sodegaura Beach" class="wp-image-3087"/></figure>
<p>From Ninomiya Fruit Park to Sodegaura Beach is a genuinely pleasant hiking distance. You cross National Route 1, duck under the Seisho Bypass, and then — boom — the sea is right in front of you.</p>
<p>That moment of walking out from under the bypass, from shadow into sun and sea air, is my favorite part. It feels a little cinematic. A little dramatic, for free.</p>
<p>The town also runs a &#8220;Ninomiya Health Walk Course.&#8221; A sign on the sidewalk lists the stats:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Distance: 3.60 km</strong></li>
<li><strong>Estimated time: 52 minutes</strong></li>
<li><strong>Calories: around 192 kcal (men) / 161 kcal (women)</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>As a middle-aged guy, I appreciate this kind of honesty. It gives me a solid excuse to daydream about how much weight I must be losing.</p>
<h2>A dog who wants to live inside the waves</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3094.jpg" alt="Momiji running along the shoreline at Sodegaura Beach" class="wp-image-3094"/></figure>
<p>The second we reached Sodegaura Beach, Momiji&#8217;s happiness meter exploded. She yanked the leash toward the water line and just went.</p>
<p>The waves were on the higher side today. White foam rushing in, pulling back, rushing in again.</p>
<p>Momiji chased the retreating waves. Then ran away from the incoming ones. Then chased again.</p>
<p>…I was worried she was overdoing it, honestly.</p>
<p>But the face a dog makes in a moment like that — eyes wide, ears flat with joy — is one of the purest things in the world. Seeing her like that, the whole trip had already paid for itself.</p>
<p>Something I&#8217;ve wondered for a while:<br /><strong>Do all dogs love water like this, or is Momiji just a weirdo about it?</strong><br />If you&#8217;re reading this and you live with a dog, I&#8217;m guessing you know what I mean.</p>
<p>If you haven&#8217;t taken your dog to the ocean or a shallow river yet, <strong>please try it once.</strong> Even somewhere shallow is fine. Watching a dog lose their mind with happiness in the water is, genuinely, one of the most healing things I know.</p>
<p>And then, something else hit me.</p>
<p>Watching Momiji charge around like a kid, <strong>I suddenly remembered my own children when they were small.</strong> Holding their tiny hands at the beach, all of them shrieking every time a wave came in.</p>
<p>Somewhere along the line, they grew up. They have their own lives now. A little lonely, and also — that&#8217;s how it&#8217;s supposed to go, so it&#8217;s okay.</p>
<p>I stood there watching Momiji chase the waves, thinking about all of that.</p>
<p>…Nostalgic. So this is what &#8220;the heart softening&#8221; feels like.<br />Momiji, you good dog.</p>
<p>Up above, a black kite (<em>tobi</em>, a coastal hawk) traced slow circles in the sky. Its whistle blended with the waves and the wind. That sound-layer alone was enough to quiet the noise in my head.</p>

<h2>Sodegaura Beach does &#8220;jibiki-ami&#8221; — something I want to try someday</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3100.jpg" alt="Jibiki-ami (traditional net fishing) poster by Ichigoro-Maru at Sodegaura Beach" class="wp-image-3100"/></figure>
<p>On the beach, there was a signboard for <strong>jibiki-ami</strong>, a traditional Japanese beach seine. I didn&#8217;t see the net being pulled today, but the sign told me this is where they do it.</p>
<p>The way it works: a long net is set out at sea by boat, and people on the shore pull it in together by hand, rope by rope, rhythm by rhythm. Everyone shouting &#8220;heave, heave&#8221; in sync.</p>
<p>The outfit running it here is called <strong>&#8220;Ichigoro-maru.&#8221;</strong> They offer beach seine experiences on Sodegaura Beach.</p>
<ul>
<li>Private group booking: about <strong>$470 USD</strong> per session (30+ people recommended)</li>
<li>Hands-free BBQ add-on: about <strong>$18 USD per adult</strong></li>
<li>You can grill your just-caught fish right there on the beach</li>
</ul>
<p>Imagine bringing your family, pulling the net together, and eating your own catch by the water.<br />…That sounds wonderful, honestly.</p>
<p>I haven&#8217;t done it yet. But if the chance comes up — <strong>I&#8217;d like to try it someday.</strong> That&#8217;s the honest version.</p>
<h2>There was a shop on the beach, too</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3101.jpg" alt="A mysterious shop space under the overpass near the beach" class="wp-image-3101"/></figure>
<p>A small shop stood near the sand. I couldn&#8217;t tell from a distance whether it was open, but it clearly had something to do with fish — the sign hinted at it.</p>
<p>Looking it up later, Sodegaura Beach does have a small infrastructure for eating fresh-caught fish right by the shore. The Ichigoro-maru BBQ plan is part of that ecosystem.</p>
<p>I didn&#8217;t go in this time. I had my dog, and something in me said: <strong>save this for next time, and do it properly.</strong></p>
<p>Keeping things in reserve — for another visit, another mood — is a small pleasure that gets more valuable as you get older.</p>
<h2>What comes back to you on the walk home</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3099.jpg" alt="Momiji happily playing in the waves" class="wp-image-3099"/></figure>
<p>Coming back, I passed under the same bypass tunnel. Same road, same light, but somehow the scenery looked different.</p>
<p>Momiji walked more calmly. I felt a little quieter too.</p>
<p>The ocean is something else. A couple of hours next to it, and the little irritations of the week start pulling back out with the tide.</p>
<p>Every so often, I just feel the urge to go see the sea.<br /><strong>Especially on clear-sky days — something about looking at the ocean clears the fog in my head.</strong></p>
<p>Is that just me? Or do you know that feeling too? I&#8217;d like to believe I&#8217;m not the only one.</p>
<h2>A gentle suggestion, if you&#8217;re tired</h2>
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img decoding="async" src="/images/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_3085.jpg" alt="The walking path near Ninomiya" class="wp-image-3085"/></figure>
<p>If you&#8217;re reading this and you&#8217;ve been feeling a little overdone, I&#8217;d like to quietly suggest:</p>
<p><strong>Find a nearby coast and walk to it.</strong></p>
<p>You don&#8217;t need to fly somewhere. You don&#8217;t need an expensive hotel. You don&#8217;t even need a big plan.</p>
<p>Park at Ninomiya Fruit Park. Walk to Sodegaura Beach. That was it, for me, and I went home a slightly better version of myself.</p>
<p>Bring your dog if you have one. Go alone if you don&#8217;t. The sea is generous either way.</p>
<p>(If you&#8217;re looking for another easy Kanagawa walk with Mt. Fuji views, try <a href="https://freelife50.com/%f0%9f%8f%94%ef%b8%8f-a-50-year-olds-gentle-hike-up-mt-ono/">Mt. Ono — a beginner-friendly hike</a>.)</p>
<h2>In one breath, a summary</h2>
<ul>
<li>Parking at Ninomiya Fruit Park is free, 28 spots</li>
<li>The walk to Sodegaura Beach is an easy hike</li>
<li>You might see a traditional beach seine (book with Ichigoro-maru if you want to try one)</li>
<li>Waves can run a bit high; coastal hawks circle overhead</li>
<li>Dogs: let the leash out and let them run — they&#8217;ll love you for it</li>
</ul>
<p>There&#8217;s no right answer. But <strong>walking a &#8220;just right&#8221; distance</strong> seems to do something quiet and useful for the mind.</p>
<p>If you&#8217;re worn out, I hope this post finds you softly.</p>
<p>That&#8217;s all for today. See you next time.</p>
