<p><strong>Introduction | Why This Frustrates Me So Much</strong></p>

<p>Japan's 2025 House of Councillors election.<br>The official voting instructions for proportional representation say:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>“You can write either the official name or the abbreviation.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>At first glance, this seems kind and convenient.<br>But in reality, this system is anything but voter-friendly.</p>

<p>For example, writing <strong>"Democratic Party"</strong> on your ballot.<br>Do you think your vote will go to the party you meant?<br>The truth is — almost all of those votes end up with the <strong>Constitutional Democratic Party (CDP)</strong>.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Chapter 1 | Write "Democratic Party," and It Goes to the CDP Anyway</h2>

<p>Both parties have officially registered the same abbreviation: <strong>“Democratic Party.”</strong></p>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><thead><tr><th>Party</th><th>Abbreviation</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Constitutional Democratic Party (CDP)</td><td>Democratic Party</td></tr><tr><td>Democratic Party for the People (DPP)</td><td>Democratic Party</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<p>So where does your "Democratic Party" vote go?</p>

<p><strong>The Reality of "Vote Splitting"</strong><br>The election commission treats ambiguous votes this way: they split them proportionally.<br>Here's how it worked in the <strong>2021 Lower House Election</strong>:</p>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><thead><tr><th>How it's written</th><th>Split ratio</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Democratic Party</td><td>About 80% → CDP / About 20% → DPP</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<p>In other words, if you write <strong>"Democratic Party," 80% of the time your vote goes to the CDP.</strong><br>Voters are not properly informed of this reality.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Chapter 2 | Why Does This Unfriendly System Still Exist?</h2>

<p><strong>Because It Benefits Politicians, Not Voters.</strong></p>

<p>If this country truly prioritized voters, it would simply:<br>✅ Ban overlapping abbreviations<br>✅ Require writing official names<br>✅ Treat ambiguous ballots as invalid</p>

<p>But that’s not the case. Why?<br>Because this messy system benefits politicians.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><thead><tr><th>Who Benefits?</th><th>What Happens</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>CDP</td><td>Over 80% of “Democratic Party” votes automatically go to them</td></tr><tr><td>DPP</td><td>Doesn’t want to give up the abbreviation; fears losing votes</td></tr><tr><td>Election Commission</td><td>No need to reform, just split the votes</td></tr><tr><td>Politicians Overall</td><td>No one wants a change that puts them at a disadvantage</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<p>In short: <strong>the more confused voters are, the better for politicians.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Chapter 3 | If This Were Truly Voter-First, It Wouldn't Look Like This</h2>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p>“Either the official name or abbreviation is fine.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>If this country really cared about voters,<br>they’d simply say:</p>

<p><strong>“Please write the official party name.”</strong></p>

<p>But they don’t, because they know confused ballots will flow toward the CDP.<br>As long as "vote splitting" exists, ignorant votes will be skewed.<br>This vague, unclear system reveals the true nature of Japanese politics.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Chapter 4 | Small Dishonesty Has Kept Japan in Stagnation for 30 Years</h2>

<p>You might think: <strong>"This is such a small, trivial issue."</strong><br>But this is exactly where Japan’s deeper problems show.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><li>Prioritizing political convenience over voter clarity</li>

<li>Leaving inconvenient systems untouched</li>

<li>Politicians more focused on their win/loss than on the people</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Small dishonesties like this have stalled Japan for decades.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Chapter 5 | At Least, Protect Your Own Vote</h2>

<p>Politicians won’t change this.<br>Don’t expect the system to improve on its own.</p>

<p>The only thing voters can do: <strong>protect their own ballots.</strong></p>

<figure class="wp-block-table"><table class="has-fixed-layout"><thead><tr><th>If you want to vote for…</th><th>Write this:</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Constitutional Democratic Party</td><td>Constitutional Democratic Party / CDP</td></tr><tr><td>Democratic Party for the People</td><td>Democratic Party for the People / DPP</td></tr></tbody></table></figure>

<p>Just remember: <strong>if you write "Democratic Party," your vote might not go where you think.</strong></p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion | This Country Won't Change by Politicians Alone — It's Voters Who Decide</h2>

<p>Leave things to politicians, and nothing will change — only what benefits them remains.<br>The only power to change the future lies in each voter’s single ballot.</p>

<p>So let’s think carefully, even about small things like this.<br>The future won’t change unless we act.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">One Last Thing</h2>

<p><strong>“It’s fine to write the abbreviation.” — Don’t fall for that line.</strong><br><strong>At least, protect your own vote.</strong></p>
